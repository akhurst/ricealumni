GIVE TO RICE

 

Who doesn't love a good summer read? Whether your summer plans will take you to the local pool or on a plane halfway across the world, you're likely to have a few good books (or their electronic equivalent) in tow.

The Association of Rice Alumni asked leading Rice faculty members and senior campus administrators for their top summer reading recommendations for Rice alumni. Our guidelines were broad: their recommendations could be related to their teaching or professional interests—or not. Non-fiction or fiction, current bestseller or classic, intellectually weighty or pure fun—anything was fair game as long as it was something they were truly interested in and appropriate for lay readers.

Their selections are below, and are sure to provide plenty of ideas for your summer reading pleasure. So from the Association of Rice Alumni, happy reading and have a terrific summer!

What We're Reading

June 1, 2010

David W. Leebron
President

-Work Hard. Be Nice: How Two Inspired Teachers Created the Most Promising Schools in America, by Jay Mathews
The Common Reading for the entering class, and the story of the founding of the KIPP schools.

-The Great American University, by Jonathan Cole

-The Last Kabbalist of Lisbon, by Richard Zimler
This was recommended by one of last year's entering students in her freshman questionnaire.

-Creating Medieval Cairo: Empire, Religion and Architectural Preservation in Nineteenth-Century Egypt, by Paula Sanders (professor of history and Rice's Dean of Graduate and Postdoctoral Studies)


Alexander X. Byrd
Associate Professor of History
2010 winner, Rice Presidential Mentoring Award

-Colonial Blackness: A History of Afro-Mexico, by Herman L. Bennett
Part of a continuing effort to puzzle out whether Mexico City and environs is as good a place to begin the history of black America as, say, Jamestown.

-So Much Reform, So Little Change: The Persistence of Failure in Urban Schools, by Charles M. Payne
I've read this a few times, but will do it again this summer. I'd support a Texas law mandating that people couldn't express their opinion of big city schools unless they also had an opinion on Payne's little, but extraordinarily bracing, book.

-Work Hard. Be Nice: How Two Inspired Teachers Created the Most Promising Schools in America, by Jay Mathews
I'm trying to figure out whether I think charter preparatory schools are indeed real schools (plus, this book is next year's Common Reading).

-The Fourth Way: The Inspiring Future for Education Change, by Andy Hargreaves and Dennis Shirley
After accountability, then what? Shirley is a former master of Will Rice College.


Justin Cronin
Professor of English and author of The Passage, the first in his apocalyptic vampire trilogy purchased by Random House and soon to be a movie by Ridley Scott.

Since I'm on a book tour most of the summer, my reading list is built around the idea of long hours on planes and waiting in airports. A number of these titles are espionage fiction, my go-to entertainment.

-Spies of the Balkans, by Alan Furst
This is on the top of my pile. He's a wonderfully stylish writer.

-I'm also reading some old Eric Ambler, Graham Greene, and John Le Carre.

Other (non-espionage) titles include:
-A Visit from the Goon Squad, by Jennifer Egan
-Every Man Dies Alone, by Hans Fallada
-Strangers at the Feast, by Jennifer Vanderbes


Rick Greenspan
Director of Athletics, Recreation, and Fitness

I read almost exclusively non-fiction with a preference for American history. I read anything John Maxwell or John Ellis write as well as anything about Teddy Roosevelt. Additionally, I read many books on leadership, management and innovation.

-Legacy of Ashes: The History of the CIA, by Tim Weiner
I am currently finishing this interesting book. This is a scary and well researched book about the birth and inadequacy of the CIA.

-My Stroke of Insight: A Brain Scientist's Personal Journey, by Jill Bolte Taylor
I plan to read this one. Jill was our neighbor in Bloomington, Indiana, and her story of recovery from a stroke at a young age and the documentation of her experiences is from a scientist's perspective. Her book is close to being developed into a major motion picture.

-George Washington’s Sacred Fire, by Peter Lillback
I am fascinated with the life of our first president and the manner in which he led without a blueprint and peacefully transferred power despite his overwhelming popularity.


Mikki Hebl
Associate Professor of Psychology
2010 winner, George R. Brown Prize for Excellence in Teaching

-Straight Man, by Richard Russo
This is an absolute must read and laugh-out-loud funny for professors or people who understand the workings of academia.

-Daisy Fay and the Miracle Man, by Fanny Flagg
Just a downright enjoyable read.

-The Count of Monte Cristo, by Alexandre Dumas
Remains my all-time favorite book.


J. Dennis Huston
Professor of English

-Emma, by Jane Austen
A book less well known than Pride and Prejudice but every bit as interesting and containing a more complex plot. Also a fine film starring Kate Beckinsale. (The Gweneth Paltrow film is not nearly as good.)

-L.A. Outlaws, by T. Jefferson Parker
A romance and a detective-fiction novel in one. He has won the Edgar Award for two of his books, one written in 2004 or 2005, with "California" in the title. I like L.A. Outlaws a lot, though—even better than the other of his Edgar Award winners, "Silent Joe."

-The Day of the Jackal, by Frederick Forsyth
A novel about forty years old, but, I think, it is the best chase novel ever written. Also a wonderful film brilliantly directed by Fred Zinnemann (who directed, among other great films, "High Noon").


Amy Jaffe
Associate Director, Rice Energy Program
Wallace Wilson Fellow, James A. Baker III Institute for Public Policy

-Three Girls and Their Brother, by Theresa Rebeck
I bought this book after seeing her play "Our House" with my son Jordan, a theatre arts major at Rice. "Our House" was an incredible satire about the news media in America and how it has become indistinguishable from entertainment reality television. I expect Rebeck to be one of the rising stars in theatre in the coming years and I am guessing Three Girls and Their Brother, which is a satire of a family that becomes famous by accident, will be a great and insightful read. (I also highly recommend the play.)

-The Pull of the Moon, by Elizabeth Berg
I heard Elizabeth Berg on NPR speaking about this book and I liked her so I figured I would like the book. The book is about women's relationships with each other as seen through the eyes of a 50 year old woman who decides to run away from home.

-Peddling Peril: How the Secret Nuclear Trade Arms America's Enemies, by David Albright
I am serving on a Congressionally funded task force on the problem of Iran's nuclear weapons program and David Albright is one of the members and perhaps the top expert in the United States on secret weapons programs. He gave me his new book Peddling Peril and I am guessing it will be a frightening but riveting read.

-Dispatches from the Edge: A Memoir of War, Disasters, and Survival, by Anderson Cooper
This book was recommended to me by a close friend (she thought I'd like it as a former journalist) and she was right. It is an insightful insider's look at what it is like to cover war and disasters as a journalist.


Elizabeth Long
Professor of Sociology and the author of Book Clubs: Women and the Uses of Reading in Everyday Life
Department Chair, Sociology

-The Botany of Desire, by Michael Pollan
Working backwards from The Omnivore's Dilemma, which I loved.

-Billy Collins poetry, any and all—a new discovery for me.

-Villa Victoria: The Transformation of Social Capital in a Boston Barrio, by Mario Small
A non-traditional ethnographic discussion of a Puerto Rican ghetto in Boston—truly well-written and a great meditation on how scholars have thought about poverty over the past 50 years, and why reality is more complex than their analyses.

-Masters of Sex: The Life and Times of William Masters and Virginia Johnson, the Couple who Taught America How to Love, by Thomas Maier
Recommended by a Rice alum!

Competing Devotions: Careers and Family Among Women Executives, by Mary Blair-Loy

Santa Olivia, by Jacqueline Carey
A dystopic view of the near future on the U.S.-Mexican border.


Sara Lowman
Vice Provost and University Librarian

-Matterhorn, by Karl Marlantes
This is an epic war story set in Vietnam—the battle scenes are very graphic and exciting (my analogy would be to the movie "Platoon" on steroids), and the character development among the best in any book that I've read.

-The Girl Who Kicked the Hornet's Next, by Stieg Larsson
This is the final book in a trilogy about a Swedish journalist and his relationship with an odd computer hacker. All three of the books in this series are excellent; this final book pulls plot lines from the previous two books together in an exciting conclusion.

-The Swan Thieves, by Elizabeth Kostova
This follow-up book to the author's debut novel, The Historian, is a great summer read. In the Swan Thieves Kostova weaves an intricate story of a mentally ill painter obsessed with a 19th-century painter. I thoroughly enjoyed the plot and the author's use of different narrators and letters from the past to move the story forward.


Allen Matusow
William Gaines Twyman Professor of History, Interim Dean of Humanities and
2010 winner, Association of Rice Alumni Gold Medal

This summer I hope to read:

-Freefall: America, Free Markets, and the Sinking of the World Economy, by Joseph Stiglitz

-The Bridge: The Life and Rise of Barack Obama, by David Remnick

-The Rebellion of Ronald Reagan: A History of the End of the Cold War, by Jim Mann


Rebecca Richards-Kortum
Stanley C. Moore Professor of Electrical and Computer Engineering
Department Chair, Bioengineering
Director, Rice 360: Institute for Global Health Technologies


-What I Thought I Knew, by Alice Eve Cohen
In this wickedly candid memoir of a late-in-life pregnancy, the author is faced with a series of heart-breaking choices. To help guide her decisions, she makes lists titled "What I Know." This intimate, ruefully funny, and suspenseful book questions whether we are ever prepared to become parents.

-A Million Miles in a Thousand Years, by Donald Miller
In this engaging memoir, Miller works with two producers who want to create a movie about his life. In shaping his story to make a more compelling film, Miller begins a journey to edit his actual life into a better story. A wonderful book with a simple, but transformational challenge—to live a better story. I loved it, although it nearly caused an existential crisis for me.

-The Three Weissmanns of Westport, by Cathleen Schine
A modern retelling of Sense & Sensibility. Set in Manhattan and Westport, Connecticut, this book is pure fun with a wonderful surprise ending.

-The Midwife: A Memoir of Birth, Joy, and Hard Times, by Jennifer Worth
Worth served as a midwife in London's east end during the 1950s. With rich humor and great tenderness, she brings to life the magic and danger of birth in the midst of extreme poverty. Reading Worth's descriptions of riding a rickety bicycle through moonlit slums to deliver babies, I was struck by the many similarities between healthcare in the postwar slums of London and the cities of Africa today. I bought so many copies of this book to give as Christmas gifts that Amazon banned me from purchasing more!


Richard Tapia
University Professor and Maxfield-Oshman Chair in Engineering

-Esalen: America and the Religion of No Religion, by Jeffrey Kripal
Kripal is the chair of the Rice Religious Studies Department. I am interested because I lived those times, California in the '60s.
