Alumni College Chicago
May 20–21, 2005

Dear Alumni and Friends,

As longtime Chicagoans and members of the Rice community, it is with great pleasure that we invite you to Alumni College Chicago, an event that celebrates the best of both a great city and a world-class university. For those who may not have experienced Alumni College, this very popular weekend of learning and camaraderie recently completed its eleventh year on the Rice campus, and attracts alumni and friends from across the country—and beyond—each year. Chicago is one of only a handful of cities to host an Alumni College "on the road," and we are especially pleased to offer an experience with a particular Chicago flair—and the involvement of Rice president David W. Leebron.

We'll kick off the weekend with a reception with President Leebron at a Chicago landmark, the Shedd Aquarium. You'll have the opportunity to visit with President Leebron as he begins to set forth his vision for Rice, and mingle with alumni and friends while enjoying private access to the aquarium. Saturday will feature the joy of discovery, the exhilaration of debate, and the pleasure of good company as we hear from outstanding speakers, including award-winning Rice professor John Boles and local Chicago experts Edward Uhlir and Ted Beattie.

A unique experience awaits: uniquely Chicago, uniquely Rice. Join us!

Bill '57 and Stephanie Sick
Vicki Whamond Bretthauer '79
Co-chairs, Alumni College Chicago

Hilton Chicago Hotel, 720 South Michigan Ave.

9:00 a.m. — Registration and coffee

9:30 a.m. — Session I
  • The U.S. Ocean Commission Report: The President's Response and Where We Go Next, Ted A. Beattie, president and CEO of the John G. Shedd Aquarium and member of the U.S. Commission on Ocean Policy
    		•

     
    After nearly three years of public meetings, site visits, and hundreds of hours of testimony and debate, the U.S. Commission on Ocean Policy presented its findings to the President and legislature in September 2004. The report included more than 200 recommendations to improve the way we manage the Great Lakes and the nation's oceans, rivers, and streams. This presentation will summarize the key Ocean Commission findings and outline how President Bush has responded to the recommendations.

    Ted A. Beattie is the president and CEO of Chicago's John G. Shedd Aquarium, which is the largest indoor aquarium in the world and attracts two million visitors annually. He earned his BA and MA from Ohio State University and has spent nearly his entire career in the zoo and aquarium world. President George W. Bush appointed Beattie to the 16-member U.S. Ocean Commission in 2000. Beattie is a recent past president of the American Zoo and Aquarium Association and currently serves as president of Chicago's major museum consortium. He also is chairman of the board of directors of the Ohio State University Alumni Association.

     
    10:45 a.m. — Break

    11:00 a.m. — Session II
  • Edgar Odell Lovett: His Education and Founding Vision for Rice, John B. Boles '65, William Pettus Hobby Professor of History, Rice University

    •  
    Edgar Odell Lovett's ambitious plans for William Marsh Rice's new institution took a vague charter and transformed the concept into an innovative plan to create a world-class university. Lovett then boldly put his plan into effect, forever shaping the character and goals of Rice University. How was he able to create such a university in the small city of Houston in 1912, a thousand miles from other great private universities and in a region not then noted for academic distinction? The lecture will discuss Edgar Odell Lovett's unusual education, his preparation for distinguished academic leadership, and his around-the-world trip. As we approach the centennial of the opening of Rice and contemplate the next century, it is instructive to recall the university's founding.

    John B. Boles is the William P. Hobby Professor of History at Rice and managing editor of the Journal of Southern History. He graduated from Rice in 1965, and is the father of two Rice graduates. He has written or edited 16 books and has lectured widely on Southern history and the history of Rice. Boles is a popular teacher and has participated in every Alumni College, speaking on a wide range of topics, including the Lewis and Clark expedition, the Bible Belt, and the 1990 Economic Summit at Rice. In 2004 he was presented with Rice's Distinguished Alumni Award and the Graduate Student Association Teaching and Mentoring Award.

     
    12:15 p.m. — Lunch

    1:45 p.m. — Session III
  • The Making of Millennium Park, Edward K. Uhlir, design director, Millennium Park
    		•

     
    The creation of Chicago's Millennium Park, widely acclaimed as some of the most exciting 25 acres in the world today, was a complex and difficult undertaking. Through an unprecedented public/private partnership with an investment of nearly $500 million, an extraordinary cultural venue was created by some of the best architects, landscape designers, and artists in the world. The class will hear how history informed the design and how the project evolved over its six-year gestation.

    Suggested activity:
    A visit to Millennium Park sometime before Alumni College Chicago.

    Edward K. Uhlir was director of research and planning at the Chicago Park District until his appointment by Mayor Richard M. Daley as design director of the Millennium Park Project. A graduate of the School of Architecture at the University of Illinois at Chicago, Uhlir received the UIC alumni association's 2002 City Partner Award, which recognizes alumni who have contributed to the vitality of the Chicago metropolitan area. He has won several other awards, including the Burnham Award for Excellence in Planning from the Metropolitan Planning Council, the American Society of Landscape Architects' National Honor Award, the Chicago Civic Federation Urban Innovation Award, and the Friends of Downtown Award for Lifetime Achievement. Uhlir is a fellow of the American Institute of Architects and the Chicago chapter presented him with their Distinguished Service Award in 1999. He is a recognized authority on park development, has lectured widely, and is co-author of "The Ark in the Park: The Story of Lincoln Park Zoo."

     
    3:00 p.m. — Break

    3:15 p.m. — Session IV
  • Detective Fiction: English versus American — Radically Different Detectives and Worlds, J. Dennis Huston, professor of English, Rice University


    • One room, many suspects, and a detective. In English detective fiction, the mystery unfolds as an orderly puzzle, where the combination of seemingly unrelated clues yields an unlikely murderer. But in an American detective story, the sleuth finds himself in a violent and chaotic world where the highest and lowest classes are connected in deceit and corruption and where the murderer is often very close indeed. This lecture will examine the darkness and complexity of American detective fiction in contrast to its more formal English cousin.

    J. Dennis Huston has taught English at Rice since 1969, offering courses on a wide variety of topics including Shakespeare, Shakespeare on film, drama, detective fiction, public speaking, and film. Huston received his BA from Wesleyan University and his MA and PhD from Yale. He has won numerous teaching prizes at Rice and was also named by the state of Texas as a Minnie Stevens Piper Professor in 2002. In 1989-90, the CASE and Carnegie Foundations named him as the National Professor of the Year.
