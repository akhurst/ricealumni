ECE and Xilinx Build Foundations for High-Performance Experimental Wireless Testbeds
Fueling next generation networks will be wireless links operating in excess of 100 Mb/s. However, even with years of progress in wireless communications, researchers (both in academia and industry) constantly struggle to test their innovations in an operational network, mostly because of the lack of a high-performance platform, which can operate at 100s of Mb/s. Ashutosh Sabharwal, Research Director of the The Department of Electrical & Computer Engineering’s Center for Multimedia Communication Research Director, Ashutosh Sabharwal and Xilinx Incorporated’s, Chris Dick are leading the task of building the high-performance platform from ground up.

Xilinx and CMC have had a fruitful relationship in the past, which included generous donations of tools and hardware to CMC's TAP (Transit Access Points) Project (funded by NSF). Based on a proposal by Sabharwal in Summer 2005, Xilinx funded several Summer Interns to work in Rice CMC Lab. The $30,000 partially funded graduate students Patrick Murphy, Chris Steger, Feifei Lou and Chirag Patel, who worked both on theoretical and experimental foundations for next generation high-performance networks. Patrick Murphy also led a team of five undergraduate students, who over the summer built many foundational components for the new platform. Two conference papers were also submitted based on the work by Chris Steger and Patrick Murphy.

Since then, Xilinx has more than doubled their gift to ECE ($40,000) in the form of 1-year Communication Fellowships and support to build next generation hardware for the new platform. According to Sabharwal, "the leadership role of Rice has not only received amazing support at Xilinx but also with our industrial and university partners, opening doors to a wide array of commercial and academic possibilities for us." As an example of that support, Rice and Xilinx University Program will jointly offer a DSP-Communications workshop, onsite at Rice in the engineering lab, February 2-3, 2006. Registrants include engineering professors from all over the world, who will come to learn about building high-performance digital signal processing and digital communications on Xilinx FPGAs.
