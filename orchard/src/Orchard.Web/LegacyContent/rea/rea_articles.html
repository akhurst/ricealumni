Rice Chemical Engineers Bring Real World Lessons Into Classroom

Many of the responsibilities and dilemmas faced by chemical engineers in the real world have nothing to do with chemical engineering. As with any job, the goal for most chemical engineers is to do the right thing the best way possible. Rice typically helps us with the "best way possible" part, however, many of us must figure out on our own what is "the right thing".

During the week of April 11th, 2005, this year's Chemical Engineering Senior Design Class received some insight from alums on two subjects where doing the right thing is paramount, yet sometimes hard to focus on - Ethics and Safety. Led by Rice alums John Perez '96 and John Germany '99, engineers from Celerity3 Engineering, Inc., served as guest lecturers to the CENG 402 class taught by Dr. Kenneth Cox. They described the roles of ethics and safety in the workplace and industry.

More specifically on the ethics content, the Celerity3 engineers spent one day describing an engineer's responsibilities and guidelines with respect to competitive bidding, political contributions, and marketing. They outlined scenarios dealing with conflict of interest and moral courage. Case studies were used to better relate the material to real-life situations.

On the safety front, the guest speakers spent two days reviewing OSHA's 29 CFR 1910.119 Process Safety Management (PSM) mandate. They reviewed the incidents that led the government to take action. They described each of the 14 elements that make up the standard and explained many of the now-common acronyms used when discussing PSM activity.

The motivation behind the effort was twofold. Two of the three founders of Celerity3 are Rice CENG alums who are dedicated to giving back to the university that gave them so much. John Perez and Robert Kreder '92 are both active with the Rice Engineering Alumni (REA) association and have worked closely with Dr. Cox since the inception of their company. With the Chemical Engineering Department up for re-accreditation, Dr. Cox thought the outside perspective and interaction with professional engineers would be a creative addition to the learning experience of the students and also serve as a step in the right direction regarding the new evaluation criteria of ABET. After some brainstorming, the group decided that Ethics and Safety were two topics students should be exposed to that are hardly covered by the current curriculum.

Process safety consultants from Celerity3 Engineering assembled the presentations and generated basic quizzes to test knowledge retention. After delivering each module and administering the quiz, the students and engineers reviewed the answers together with supplemental discussion.

According to Dr. Cox, the feedback from students has been positive. They found the lectures to be quite informative and a nice change of pace from the usual syllabus. Celerity3 and Dr. Cox are already planning additional topics for the Fall semester.
