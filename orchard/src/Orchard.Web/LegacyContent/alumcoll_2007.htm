Rice University Alumni College Weekend
February 23–25, 2007

What is Alumni College Weekend?

Since 1995, the Association of Rice Alumni has held an annual event inviting alumni and friends back to campus for a weekend of classes from Rice's outstanding faculty. We firmly believe that the pursuit of knowledge should be lifelong, and Alumni College is a fabulous opportunity to sample the most recent research and top-notch teaching of the university. And when you're not in class, Alumni College offers you a chance to mingle at meals and social events with your fellow "classmates" — a broad spectrum of Rice alumni from various class years and regions, plus parents of current students and other knowledge-seeking friends. Read through the course descriptions, decide which classes interest you most, and sign up today!

The 2007 Program

The classes offered at Alumni College are grouped into tracks — themes that help you identify topics of particular interest. This year we are pleased to present the following tracks:

Energy — A broad overview of the issues surrounding energy resources and conservation for the future

Immigration — A look at the history of migratory labor and U.S. immigration and the effects on the community .

Computers and Cyberspace — How machines and the Internet are changing human activities and interaction

International Affairs — A discussion of U.S. relationships with foreign governments and international organizations

Spotlight on Alumni Instructors — A showcase of four of the many outstanding Rice University alumni academics

Potpourri — An exciting mixture of courses covering university policy, art, music, and literature

For more information on the Alumni College program, please contact Lauren Linn at 713-348-6093.

  • Hitching a Ride on a Comet: The Exhilarating Experience of Working with Rick Smalley, Robert F. Curl '54
    		•

    Spotlight on Alumni Instructors

    In October 2005, the Rice community suffered the untimely and painful loss of Rice professor Richard Smalley. Besides bringing glory to Rice, Smalley had a profoundly transformative effect on the institution. Professor Robert Curl, Smalley's research partner and longtime friend, will describe what it was like to work with him through the discovery of fullerenes.

    Suggested readings:
    Hugh Aldersey-Williams, The Most Beautiful Molecule: The Discovery of the Buckyball (1995).
    Jim E. Baggott, Perfect Symmetry: The Accidental Discovery of Buckminsterfullerene (1995).
    Boris I. Yakobson and Richard Smalley, "Fullerene Nanotubes: C1,000,000 and Beyond," American Scientist (July-August 1997). Available online through the Digital Library.

    Robert F. Curl received his BA in chemistry from the Rice Institute in 1954. He earned his PhD at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1957, and was a research fellow at Harvard University from 1957–1958. He began his teaching career at Rice as an assistant professor in 1958, became an associate professor in 1963, and a full professor in 1967. He served as master of Lovett College from 1968–1972 and chair of the chemistry department from 1992–1996. Jointly with Richard Smalley and British chemist Harold Kroto, Curl received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1996. The Association of Rice Alumni named Curl a Distinguished Alumnus in 1997.

  • Expectations of Privacy and Data Security in a Networked World, Chris Bronk
    		•

    Computers and Cyberspace

    Data manipulation, phishing scams, identity theft, direct mail, cookies — this set of phenomena describe the pitfalls that threaten the information economy, which once promised an unimaginably bright future delivered by networked computers. This lecture discusses the issues produced by information technologies developing far more rapidly than the policies designed to regulate them. We will cover expectations of online privacy, the requisites of organizational information stewardship, and individual responsibilities as users of the Internet.

    Chris Jay Hoofnagle, "Privacy Self-Regulation: A Decade of Disappointment," Electronic Privacy Information Center (March 4, 2005).
    Gary Rivlin, "Keeping Your Enemies Close," New York Times (November 12, 2006).
    Consumer Privacy Protection Act of 2002, HR 4678, 109th Congress, 2nd session.

    Follow-up readings:
    Amitai Etzioni, The Limits of Privacy (1999).
    Bruce Schneier, Secrets and Lies: Digital Security in a Networked World (2000).
    Daniel J. Solove, The Digital Person: Technology and Privacy in the Information Age (2004).

    A fellow in technology, society and public policy for the James A. Baker III Institute for Public Policy, Chris Bronk possesses experience in government information technology, software development, and public administration. Before joining Rice, he served as a foreign service officer with the U.S. State Department in domestic and overseas assignments, including in the Office of eDiplomacy, an internal think tank dedicated to the adoption of transformational technologies for the conduct of foreign affairs. He received his doctorate from Syracuse University's Maxwell School of Citizenship and Public Affairs in 2005. Earlier in his academic career, he studied international relations at Oxford University and received a bachelor's degree from the University of Wisconsin–Madison.

  • The Changing Geopolitics of Energy, Amy Myers Jaffe
    		•

    Energy

    International relations and regional political trends in the Middle East and South America have become increasingly important factors influencing energy prices and the energy strategies of major oil consuming countries such as the United States, China, India, and the nations of the European Union. This lecture will introduce the major geopolitical trends influencing the price of oil, covering such current events such as the Israeli-Lebanese conflict; U.S. policy towards Iraq; Iran's resurgence as a regional power in the Middle East; the future of Saudi Arabia; and U.S. foreign policy.

    Suggested readings:
    "Survey: Oil in troubled waters," The Economist (April 30, 2005). Available online through the Digital Library.
    Jan H. Kalicki and David L. Goldwyn, eds., Energy and Security: Toward a New Foreign Policy Strategy (2005).

    Amy Myers Jaffe is associate director of the Rice Energy Program and the Wallace S. Wilson Fellow for Energy Studies at the James A. Baker III Institute for Public Policy. Her research focuses on the subjects of oil geopolitics, strategic energy and energy science policy, and energy economics. Jaffe received the 1994 Award for Excellence from the International Association for Energy Economics and is a member of the Council on Foreign Relations. In 2005, she was honored as one of Esquire's "100 Best and Brightest." Jaffe is widely published, and is a principal author of the Baker Institute's 15 energy studies, which cover energy policy and trends in the United States, Middle East, Caspian Basin, China, and Japan, as well as emerging technologies in the nuclear, nanotechnology, and natural gas sectors.

  • Intimacy with Machines in the Digital Age, G. Anthony Gorry
    		•

    Computers and Cyberspace

    Our notions about machines reach back to the Industrial Revolution. We realize that machines have the power to relieve us of drudgery and enrich our material lives, but fear that they also hold the potential to oppress and dehumanize us. Over the past century, however, new technologies have emerged that test our notions of what machines are and how we relate to them. Today our best machines, of which the computer is the most prominent, erode the boundaries between nature and technology, reality and artifice, mind and body, and person and machine. This course will discuss the emergence of these machines and suggest some of the ways in which they are changing who we are and what we may become.

    Tony Gorry is the Friedkin Professor of Management and a professor of computer science at Rice, where he heads the Center for Technology in Teaching and Learning and directs a National Library of Medicine Training Grant in medical informatics and computational biology. Gorry is also an adjunct professor of neuroscience at Baylor College of Medicine, a member of the Institute of Medicine of the National Academy of Sciences, and a fellow of the American College of Medical Informatics.

  • Music and the Mind, Anthony K. Brandt
    		•

    Potpourri

    The scientific exploration of the human mind is relatively young and brain imaging technology is very new. Yet the arts and philosophy have been researching the mind for thousands of years — how we think; how we feel; what we notice, care about, and remember; how we search for and recognize patterns, and ascribe meaning to them. Thus, a partnership between the science and the arts offers one of the richest ways to investigate the mind. Because music is abstract, non-verbal, and universal to human culture, it has the potential to offer especially powerful insights into how we think, remember, and relate.

    An associate professor of composition and theory at Rice's Shepherd School of Music, Anthony Brandt began his career with degrees from the California Institute of Arts and Harvard University. He is co-founder and president of the acclaimed contemporary music ensemble Musiqa. Brandt has received Rice's Phi Beta Kappa Teaching Prize and the Access to Artistic Excellence Grant from the National Endowment for the Arts.

  • Nanotechnology and Energy — Be a Scientist, Save the World, Wade Adams
    		•

    Energy

    As demand for energy increases to fuel advancing technologies and markets, the need to develop new sources of power that are widespread, cheap, and clean will become ever more acute. Solving this problem will demand revolutionary breakthroughs in the physical sciences and engineering, particularly in nanotechnology. This class will briefly describe how nanotechnology revolutions might be able to address the energy problem. We will also examine workforce issues surrounding new technologies and address how Houston must change to retain its dominance in the energy industry.

    David Goodstein, Out of Gas: The End of the Age of Oil (2004).
    Jennifer Kahn, "Nano's Big Future," National Geographic (June 2006).

    (October 9, 2006).

    Tim Appenzeller, "The End of Cheap Oil," National Geographic (June 2004).
    "Special Issue: Energy's Future Beyond Carbon," Scientific American (September 2006). Available online through the Digital Library.
    Richard Booker, Nanotechnology for Dummies (2005).
    "Energy and Nanotechnology: Strategy for the Future" conference materials and webcast (Rice University, May 2003).
    Rick Smalley, "Our Energy Challenge" webcast (Columbia University, September 23, 2003).

    Wade Adams is the director of the Richard E. Smalley Institute for Nanoscale Science and Technology at Rice. Adams retired in January 2002 from his position as chief scientist of the Materials and Manufacturing Directorate at the Wright-Patterson Air Force Base in Dayton, Ohio. For the past 33 years, Adams has conducted research in polymer physics, concentrating on structure-property relations in high-performance organic materials. He was educated at the U.S. Air Force Academy, Vanderbilt University, and the University of Massachusetts.   • A Sustainable Building Program in Houston, Rives T. Taylor '82
    		•

    Energy

    Buildings use one-third of all the energy and two-thirds of all electricity consumed in the United States. Thus, the challenge of sustainability is to design buildings that use the least amount of nonrenewable energy possible, produce little or no pollution, and minimize the cost of energy dollars — while at the same time creating profitable and healthy places to live and work. This class describes existing building conditions in Houston and examines the city's first steps toward a sustainable building program.

    A lecturer with Rice's School of Architecture, Rives Taylor is a Texas-licensed, practicing architect and educator. He leads the sustainable project delivery area for the design firm Gensler. He is on the board of the Rice Design Alliance — sitting on its program, editorial, and fund-raising committees — and is active in the local chapter of the American Institute of Architects. Both at Rice and as an adjunct professor at the University of Houston, Taylor teaches sustainable design methodology, architectural history and theory, and architectural research methods. For his efforts in advancing sustainable design in the Houston region he was recognized in 2005 with the President's Citation of the Houston Citizen's Environmental Coalition.
     

  • Sanctions and Foreign Policy: Economic Coercion Really Can Work, Sometimes, T. Clifton Morgan
    		•

    International Affairs

    Most people believe that economic sanctions are ineffective instruments of foreign policy, yet many governments (especially the United States) increasingly use or threaten to use this tactic. However, some studies suggest that sanctions are perhaps far more potent than is widely believed. This course will outline alternate methods of evaluating sanctions policy and also consider which factors may make sanctions more likely to work.

    Suggested reading:
    T. Clifton Morgan and Valerie L. Schwebach, "Fools Suffer Gladly: The Use of Economic Sanctions in International Crises," International Studies Quarterly (March 1997).

His research centers on the study of international conflict and bargaining, foreign policy theory, and the efficacy of economic sanctions. Morgan wrote the book Untying the Knot of War in 1994, earning him an American Political Science Association book award and the International Studies Association's 1994 Karl Deutsch Award for his contributions to the study of international relations and peace. His most recent book is A Theory of Foreign Policy, which he co-authored with Glenn Palmer in 2006.

    		•

    The lecture will address how U.S. dependence on imported oil shapes our policies toward major energy exporters. Barnes's work has appeared in the New York Times, Houston Chronicle, Oil and Gas Journal, Energy Markets, the Newsletter of the Royal United Services Institute, the SAIS Policy Forum Series, Survival, and the National Interest. Barnes is a contributor to two recent books, Energy in the Caspian Region and Natural Gas and Geopolitics from 1970 to 2040. From 1979–1993, Barnes was a diplomat with the U.S. State Department. He is a graduate of Princeton University.

    Immigration

    This course will trace out from the beginning of the twentieth century a Mexican-American literary response to the topic of immigration. A survey that includes ballads and folklore will demonstrate how immigration to the United States through Texas captures the imagination of successive generations of writers of Mexican descent. This course will endeavor to offer a recorded view of immigration often elided by history and the politics of race. The stories recorded here are often more complicated and human than one imagines.

    José F. Aranda Jr., associate professor of Chicano and American literature at Rice, received his BA from Yale University and his MA and PhD in American literature from Brown University. He is the author of When We Arrive: A Literary History of Mexican America, and has begun work on his next book, tentatively titled The Places of Modernity in Early Mexican American Literature, 1840-1960. Aranda recently completed a three-year appointment by the Modern Language Association Executive Council to the Committee on the Literatures of People of Color. He is the co-coordinator for the Mellon Mays Undergraduate Fellows Program at Rice and is a founding member of Chicano Leadership Rice. Aranda and his wife, English assistant professor Krista Comer, are masters of Baker College.
     

  • Rethinking the Pantheon, Caroline Quenemoen
    		•

    Potpourri

    The 2,000-year-old Pantheon stands as a testament to the brilliance of Roman concrete technology. Despite the building's iconic status in Western architecture, it remains the most misunderstood monuments of antiquity. This lecture will reveal that the Pantheon was not a temple to all the gods, as its name implies, and that it was not a design failure, as artists and scholars since the Renaissance have argued.

    Caroline Quenemoen is an assistant professor of art history and classical studies. She received her PhD in classical archaeology from Yale University in 2001. Her research focuses on Roman art and architecture of the late republic and Augustan periods. In her current book project, she examines the formation of Augustan identity in one of his earliest and most extensive building projects in Rome: his house and temple complex on the Palatine hill. She teaches courses in both Greek and Roman art and has organized study-abroad courses for Rice students in Italy and Greece.

  • Of eBay, MySpace, and YouTube: The Internet's Influence on Consumer Behavior, Paul M. Dholakia
    		•

    Computers and Cyberspace

    Over the last 15 years, the emergence of the Internet has empowered consumers, yet created an overload of information in virtually every product category. This class will consider various aspects of the Internet's influence on consumer decision-making and choice behavior. In the session, we will examine consumers' decision-making and buying behaviors on eBay; the reasons for the emergence of virtual brand communities; and how customer community marketing programs are changing the way companies market to their customers.

  • Houston in Transition: Toward a New Politics of Energy and the Environment in the 21st Century, Stephen L. Klineberg
    		•

    Energy

    Drawing on 25 years of systematic surveys, this class will track the transformation of Houston's economy and demographics from the oil boom years as America's quintessential "free-enterprise, anti-government" city through the economic collapse of the 1980s and into the new millennium. To attract and retain the nation's most innovative companies and talented individuals, Houston will now need to become a more appealing urban destination and develop the new modes of energy production to fuel economic growth. We will assess the uneven progress that Houston has made in this regard examine the extent to which a "new politics" of energy and the environment is actually taking place in the Houston area today.

    Suggested reading:
    Stephen L. Klineberg, Public Perceptions in Remarkable Times: Tracking Change Through 24 Years of Houston Surveys (2005).

    A graduate of Haverford College near Philadelphia, sociology professor Stephen L. Klineberg received an MA in psychopathology from the University of Paris and a PhD in social psychology from Harvard University. After teaching at Princeton, he joined Rice's faculty in 1972. In March 1982, he and his students initiated the annual Houston Area Survey, now beginning its second quarter-century of measuring systematically the demographic patterns, attitudes, and life experiences of area residents. The recipient of nine major teaching awards at Rice, Klineberg has written numerous journal articles, appears frequently on radio and television, and publishes a continually updated series of reports on the survey findings.
