Faculty Lecturers in the Humanities

John Boles '65, History

William P. Hobby Professor of History and managing editor of the Journal of Southern History

Scholarly interests: U.S. Southern history and history of Rice University
Achievements: written or edited 16 books; many teaching awards; ARA distinguished alumni award
 

  • "The Enigma of Thomas Jefferson"
    		•

    Thomas Jefferson was an extraordinarily gifted, energetic, creative, and conflicted person, who at times seems a bundle of contradictions. This lecture will attempt to make sense of this multitalented man, understanding him in terms of the context of his age and evaluating his contribution to American history. As both a slave owner and an eloquent defender of liberty, he embodies the contradictions and promise of this nation.
     

  • "The Education of a President: Edgar Odell Lovett and the Making of Rice University"
    		•

    The butler did it. Captain Baker to the rescue. Lovett's 'round-the-world adventures during his first year as president. We've all taken the campus tour, seen "The Trust," or somehow put together enough random bits of Rice lore over the years that we know the Rice story, right? Think again.
     


    Leo Costello,     Art History

    Assistant professor of art history

    Scholarly interests: European art
     
  • "Monet and the Impressionists at Argenteuil"
    		•

    The Parisian suburb of Argenteuil has secured a place in art history as perhaps the single most important site of Impressionism. It was here during the 1870s that Claude Monet and his colleagues came to artistic and professional maturity, creating some of the most enduring masterpieces of the Impressionist movement. This lecture will explore the pictorial, personal, and historical significance of this site. In particular, we will look at the work of Monet, noting the importance of Argenteuil as a varied motif for his work and a stable base for his personal and professional aspirations.
     


    Christian J. Emden,     German

    Assistant professor of German; co-director of Focus Europe, a new undergraduate program, and Changing Perspectives of the Public Sphere, a two-year collaboration between the Rice School of Humanities and the University of Cambridge.

    Scholarly interests: German intellectual history
     
  • "War, Morality, and Modernity"
    		•

    While much recent discussion about the nature of war is limited to questions concerned with public policy, it is often forgotten that — especially in the European tradition — thinking about war is also part of wider cultural and intellectual processes on the path to modernity. This lecture will focus on the changes that the cultural perception and intellectual discussion of war and organized violence have undergone, drawing on both political philosophy and media studies.

    J. Dennis Huston,     English

    Professor of English

    Scholarly interests: Shakespeare, drama, public speaking, film
    Achievements: numerous teaching prizes at Rice; state of Texas as a Minnie Stevens Piper Professor (2002); Council for Advancement and Support of Education and the Carnegie Foundation National Professor of the Year (1989-1990).
     
  • "Movies: The Language of Film"
    		•

    Drawing from films as different as My Fair Lady, Romeo and Juliet, The Third Man, Dirty Dancing, Hamlet, Witness, The Graduate, and Crimson Tide, Professor Huston will talk about the way directors use the many elements of film to convey meaning. He will show clips from movies and talk about the way costumes, makeup, camera angles, blocking, focus, set, cutting, lighting, and color suggest thematic meaning, often at a subconscious level, to an audience watching these films.

    Allen Matusow,     History

    William Gaines Twyman Professor of History; associate director for academic programs at the Baker Institute

    Scholarly interests: Foreign policy of Nixon and Kissinger; post World War II American foreign policy and U.S. history 1945-1974
    Achievements: Dean of humanities 1981-1995; several teaching awards
     
  • "Did Reagan Win the Cold War?"
    		•

    The most astonishing event of the post-World War II Era was the sudden end of the Cold War. In 1983 President Ronald Reagan, in the midst of the largest military build-up in peacetime American history, called the Soviet Union "the evil empire." In 1988, during his summit meeting in Moscow with Mikhail Gorbachev, Reagan said of his remark, "I was talking about another time, another era." This lecture will try to assess Reagan's policies during the 1980s to answer this question: Did he force the end of the evil empire?
     

    Wesley Morris,     English

    Professor of English, Hanszen master

    Scholarly interests: Twentieth-century literary theory; William Faulkner
    Achievements: Former department chair
     
  • "Breaking the Frame: The Story of Three Postmodern Pigs"
    		•

    There are more than seventy-five version of The Three Little Pigs available today, but are they all alike? What if the three pigs broke free of their story and wandered into other tales? Would they still be the same little pigs? Would the story be the same story so many of us know and love? This class will provide answers to these questions by looking at David Wiesner's prize-winning book, The Three Pigs.

    Anthony Pinn,     Humanities, Religious Studies

    Agnes Cullen Arnold Professor of the Humanities; professor of religious studies

    Scholarly interests: liberation theologies, African American religious thought, religion and popular culture, and African American humanism
     
  • "Malcolm X and the Nation of Islam"
    		•

    Any discussion of the Nation of Islam will turn to its most well-known leader, Malcolm X. His efforts and ministry transformed the Nation of Islam and greatly expanded its prominence. This class will survey the history and theology of the Nation of Islam and trace its development, focusing on the work of Malcolm X.
     

    Caroline Quenemoen,     Art History

    Assistant professor of art history

    Scholarly interests: Roman art and architecture of the late republic and Augustan periods
     
  • "Rethinking the Pantheon"
    		•

    The 2,000-year-old Pantheon stands as a testament to the brilliance of Roman concrete technology. Despite the building's iconic status in Western architecture, it remains the most misunderstood monuments of antiquity. This lecture will reveal that the Pantheon was not a temple to all the gods, as its name implies, and that it was not a design failure, as artists and scholars since the Renaissance have argued.
     

    Carol Quillen,     History

    Associate professor of history, vice-provost for academic affairs, and director of Rice's Boniuk Center for the Study and Advancement of Religious Tolerance.

    Scholarly interests: European humanism and feminist political theory.
    Achievements: George R. Brown Award for Superior Teaching (3 times)
     
  • "In Defense of Tolerance: The Mission of the Boniuk Center"
    		•

    Tolerance as an ideal finds few strong advocates. Those who value religious diversity argue that we should delight in and learn from our differences, rather than simply tolerate them. For others, tolerance means condoning acts they know to be wrong or beliefs they know to be false. This lecture will explore how a rich understanding of and an active commitment to tolerance can foster peaceful co-existence among persons of profoundly different religious backgrounds.
     

    Lori Wildenthal '87,     History

    Associate professor of history

    Scholarly interests: human rights
     
  • "Torture in Historical Perspective"
    		•

    This lecture will discuss how the place of torture in the European legal system has changed through the years and then focus on the distinctive nature of state-sponsored torture in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries worldwide.
     
  • "What are 'Human Rights'?"
    		•

    The language of international human rights has become ever more prominent since the Second World War – and especially so in recent years. This lecture will offer an introduction to the historical setting in which that language emerged, a discussion of the helpful and problematic aspects of describing situations in terms of human rights and a sketch of recent debates over expanding human rights norms to address not only individuals but also groups other than nation states.
