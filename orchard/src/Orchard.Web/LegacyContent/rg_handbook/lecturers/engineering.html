Faculty Lecturers in Engineering

Wade Adams, Nanotechnology

Director of the Richard E. Smalley Institute for Nanoscale Science and Technology
 

  • "Nanotechnology and Energy – Be a Scientist, Save the World"
    		•

    As demand for energy increases to fuel advancing technologies and markets, the need to develop new sources of power that are widespread, cheap, and clean will become ever more acute. Solving this problem will demand revolutionary breakthroughs in the physical sciences and engineering, particularly in nanotechnology. This class will briefly describe how nanotechnology revolutions might be able to address the energy problem. We will also examine workforce issues surrounding new technologies and address how Houston must change to retain its dominance in the energy industry.
     


    Rebekah Drezek,     Bioengineering, Electrical and Computer Engineering

    Associate professor of bioengineering and associate professor in electrical and computer engineering

    Scholarly interests: the development of minimally invasive imaging approaches for the detection, diagnosis, and monitoring of disease, especially cancer
    Achievements: first engineering to receive a M.D. Anderson Odyssey Fellowship (2001-2002); MIT Technology Review’s list of the world’s top 100 young innovators (2004); Rice’s graduate teaching and mentoring award (2004).
     
  • "Nanotechnology in Medicine"
    		•

    With nanotechnology, we can construct tiny molecules and tailor them for specific purposes. For medicine, the nanomaterials are so small that they can enter blood cells and travel through blood vessels, giving scientists new methods of detecting and diagnosing disease, particularly cancer. This class will introduce you to the principles and possibilities of this exciting new field.
     


    Antonios Mikos,     Bioengineering

    John W. Cox Professor of Bioengineering and Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering

    Scholarly interests: synthesis of new biomaterials for tissue engineering as carriers for controlled drug delivery and as non-viral vectors for gene therapy.
    Achievements: 2007 O'Donnell Award from The Academy of Medicine, Engineering and Science of Texas (TAMEST); Orthopaedic Research Society's 2005 Marshall R. Urist Award for Excellence in Tissue Regeneration Research; holds 19 patents.
     
  • "Tissue Engineering at Rice"
    		•

    Rice's Center for Excellence in Tissue Engineering (CETE) focuses on the science and technology of tissue engineering and the development of new means of replacing damaged or diseased body parts and restoring function. This presentation will discuss the research and educational activities of CETE and the promise of the field to revolutionize health care in the future.
     

    Moshe Vardi,     Computational Engineering

    Karen Ostrum George Professor in Computational Engineering and director of the Computer and Information Technology Institute (CITI) at Rice

    Scholarly interests: applications of logic to computer science, including database theory, finite-model theory, knowledge in multi-agent systems, computer-aided verification and reasoning, and teaching logic across the curriculum
    Achievements: President of the International Federation of Computational Logicians; 2002, elected into the U.S. National Academy of Engineering; 2002, elected into the European Academy of Sciences; 2005, awarded a Guggenheim fellowship
     
  • "Who Started the Information Revolution?"
    		•

    The revolution begun by the development of the silicon chip radically altered the technological tools of our lives and launched the information age. This lecture will investigate the origins of this revolution and the inventors and visionaries involved.
