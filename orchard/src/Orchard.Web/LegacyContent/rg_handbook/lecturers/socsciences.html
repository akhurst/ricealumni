Faculty Lecturers in the Social Sciences

John Alford, Political Science

Associate professor of political science

Scholarly interests: elections and voting, biology and political behavior
Achievements: expert witness for Texas Congressional redistricting (2001, 2003)
 

  • "The Biology of Human Political Behavior: Genetics, Evolution, and the Political Brain"
    		•

    The phrases "Once a Democrat, always a Democrat" and "Once a Republican, always a Republican" may be truer than we realize. Our identification with a political party, which we acquire from early childhood socialization, often does last a lifetime. According to Dr. Alford, Americans' political orientation – liberal or conservative – is a genetic trait almost as unlikely to change as our eye color.
     


    Joe Barnes,     Baker Institute

    Baker Institute fellow

    Scholarly interests: geopolitics of energy
     
  • "Energy and U.S. Foreign Policy: Saudi Arabia, Russia, and Iran"
    		•

    The lecture addresses how U.S. dependence on imported oil shapes our policies toward major energy exporters. It will focus on three countries: Saudi Arabia is a long-time friend reassessing its relationship with the United States; Russia is a resurgent power that sees high energy prices as a means to flex its muscle on the international stage; and Iran is a "rogue state" in confrontation with Washington and the world community.
     


    Amy Myers Jaffe,     Baker Institute

    Wallace S. Wilson Fellow for Energy Studies and associate director of the Rice Energy Program at the James A. Baker III Institute for Public Policy

    Scholarly interests: oil geopolitics, strategic energy and energy science policy, and energy economics
    Achievements: 1994 Award for Excellence from the International Association for Energy Economics; Esquire magazine's 2005 "100 Best and Brightest"
     
  • "The Changing Geopolitics of Energy"
    		•

    International relations and regional political trends in the Middle East and South America have become increasingly important factors influencing energy prices and the energy strategies of major oil consuming countries such as the United States, China, India, and the nations of the European Union. This lecture introduces the major geopolitical trends influencing the price of oil, covering such current events such as the Israeli-Lebanese conflict; U.S. policy towards Iraq; Iran's resurgence as a regional power in the Middle East; the future of Saudi Arabia; and U.S. foreign policy.
     

    Neal Lane,     Baker Institute

    University Professor with appointments in physics and astronomy and Senior Fellow at the James A. Baker III Institute

    Scholarly interests: science and technology policy
    Achievements: Director of the National Science Foundation (1993-1998) and the President's science advisor and director of the White House Office of Science and Technology Polics (1998-2001)
     
  • "Albert Einstein: Lost in Space-Time"
    		•

    Albert Einstein, arguably the most famous scientist of all time, made several discoveries that shook physics – his special theory of relatively placed physics in a four-dimensional space (called space-time) and produced the most famous equation in physics, E=mc2; his general theory of relatively changed the Newtonian paradigm by replacing gravitational forces with a geometric warping of space-time; and his quantum theory of light was key to the development, by others, of quantum mechanics, the truth of which Einstein never accepted. This lecture will describe several of Einstein's discoveries, but without mathematical rigor; review his personal life, the "ups" and "downs;" and attempt to define the impact this one man had on war, peace, and civilization.
     


    Lyn Ragsdale,     Political Science

    Dean of the School of Social Sciences, Radoslav A. Tsanoff Chair of Public Affairs, and professor of political science

    Scholarly interests: the presidency, electoral behavior, and Congress
     
  • "President's Tough Choices"
    		•

    Presidents' major decisions define their terms in office. Newspaper headlines flash the dramatic choices: Truman drops the bomb; Johnson passes civil rights; Ford pardons Nixon. These actions secure the presidents' places in history, whether their standings are heroic, ignoble, or inconsequential. This talk examines how presidents make these major decisions and focuses on the competition that unfolds among advisors to give the president the choice he ultimately selects.
     


    Richard Stoll,     Political Science

    Professor of political science and associate dean of the School of Social Sciences

    Scholarly interests: international conflict, American national security policy and the use of computer simulations
    Achievements: author of six books; winner of nine teaching awards at Rice
     
  • "The Impact of an Iraqi War Protest Movement: What Lessons Can We Learn from Vietnam?"
    		•

    Cindy Sheehan's protest in Crawford, Texas, against U.S. involvement in Iraq has drawn much attention. Some have wondered if we will see a protest movement emerge — as witnessed during the Vietnam War — and whether this anti-war movement will have an impact on U.S. policy in Iraq in the way that public opinion affected U.S. policy with regard to Vietnam. But did the Vietnam protest movement really effect a change in U.S. policy? This lecture examines the evidence and discusses the implications for today.
     
  • "Preemptive Aggression: Assessing the Use of Military Force by the Bush Administration"
    		•

    The Bush Administration has proclaimed a doctrine of preemption for situations in which there are emerging threats. This doctrine led to the recent war against Iraq. Does this doctrine make sense? Can we say that the use of military force in Iraq and Afghanistan has been successful? Are there (and should there be) other components to our national security policy towards Iraq, Afghanistan and other "rogue states"?
