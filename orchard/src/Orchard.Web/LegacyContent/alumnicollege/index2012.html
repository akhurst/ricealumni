Rice University Alumni College Weekend
March 9-11, 2012

What is Alumni College Weekend?

Welcome to Rice's 18th annual Alumni College Weekend! Since 1995, the Association of Rice Alumni has held this weekend-long event inviting alumni, parents, and friends to campus for an experience that is uniquely Rice.  Join us for a series of classes from some of our best and brightest professors and rising stars March 9-11, 2012.  The Association of Rice Alumni firmly believes that the pursuit of knowledge should continue beyond your time as a student, and Alumni College Weekend is a fabulous opportunity to expand your intellectual horizons.  And when you’re not in class, the program offers a chance to mix, mingle, and network with your fellow “classmates” — a broad spectrum of Rice community members. 

The 2012 Program

You'll notice icons next to each of the course descriptions throughout this brochure. The classes offered at Alumni College are grouped into tracks: themes that help you identify topics of particular interest. This year we are pleased to present the following tracks:

  Take a Break
An often requested chance to have light exercise during an otherwise purely academic weekend.
  The Centennial
Classes dealing with the people and actions surrounding the founding Rice as we honor our centennial year.
  A Century in the Making
Topics from biology to architecture, music to physics: What were they like in 1912 and how have they changed.
  Biography of Earth
A broad look at the Earth and the people who inhabit it

B. Paul Padley
Progress Through Failure, A Century of Physics

A Century in the Making

If you have ever taken a physics class, you can rest assured that whatever you learned was wrong to some extent.  In fact our greatest leaps forward come when what we believe to be true is shown to fail to explain nature correctly.   We will look at past failures, including some examples with a Rice connection.  It turns out that we can prove quite conclusively that currently we are failing to describe 95% of the universe! We must be on the verge of a really big leap forward in our understanding of nature.

B. Paul Padley, PhD, is professor of physics and astronomy at Rice University, where he conducts research in experimental particle physics in an attempt to understand the basic constituents of matter and the laws of nature that govern them. He has worked on experiments at several of the world's leading particle physics facilities and currently is working on the Large Hadron Collider at CERN. He has also been active in bringing physics to the public, contributing to an episode of the Magic Schoolbus and giving advice to programs and appearing on the Discovery Channel.

Walter Bailey
Modern Music in 1912

A century in the making

What constituted “modern music” in 1912?  Works such as Arnold Schoenberg’s Pierrot lunaire, Claude Debussy’s Jeux, and compositions by American composers Henry Cowell and Charles Ives, set the bar for musical modernism that year.  What did audiences in the United States think about such music, and how did the music played at the opening festivities of the Rice Institute reflect these perceptions of musical modernism?  With the help of music critics, commentators, and other journalists published in 1912, we can get a sense of “what modern was.”

Walter Bailey is Associate Professor and Chair of Musicology at Rice University’s Shepherd School of Music.  He received a bachelor of music in composition from Lewis and Clark College and master of arts and Ph.D. degrees in musicology from the University of Southern California. He joined the Shepherd School faculty in 1982. He has taught a variety of courses for undergraduate and graduate students, including surveys, period studies, and seminars on the music of Schoenberg, Stravinsky, Strauss, Satie, and on American art music.  His research interests include American and European music from the early 1900s.

Bailey has received grants and fellowships from the National Endowment for the Humanities and Rice University.  He is the author of Programmatic Elements in the Works of Schoenberg (UMI Research Press, 1984), coauthor (with Nancy Gisbrecht Bailey) of Radie Britain:  A Bio-Bibliography (Greenwood Press, 1990), and editor of The Schoenberg Companion (Greenwood Press, 1998), to which he also contributed several articles.  His articles and reviews have appeared in a number of journals and collections, including Arnold Schönberg—Interpretationen seiner Werke (Laaber Verlag, 2002), The Cambridge Companion to Schoenberg (2010), the Journal of the Arnold Schoenberg Institute, the Musical Quarterly, Notes, the Journal of Southern History, American Music, and the Journal of the Society for American Music.

Allison Meyer Vann
Pilates for Beginners

Take a Break

Pilates is a body conditioning routine that helps build flexibility and long, lean muscles, strength and endurance in the legs, abdominals, arms, hips, and back. It puts emphasis on spinal and pelvic alignment, breathing to relieve stress and allow adequate oxygen flow to muscles, developing a strong core or center (tones abdominals while strengthening the back), and improving coordination and balance. 

Alison Vann has been a student of classical Pilates for over 13 years.  She is certified in the mat work and the equipment through Peak Pilates.  Alison is committed to sharing her love of Pilates and movement to people of all ages and fitness levels.  She  is a practicing attorney and lives in Southampton with her husband.

Wesley Morris
The Challenge of Teaching “American Fiction 1910 to 1950”

A Century in the Making

It is not surprising, that in Morris' 40+ years of teaching American fiction the course has changed significantly.  This change is much more than a matter of what texts are taught.  There is still significant focus on classical texts by Ernest Hemingway, F. Scott Fitzgerald, and William Faulkner, but new texts have been added over the years: Kate Chopin, Jean Toomer, and John Dos Passos to name a few.  The change, however, is not merely what is taught, but how Morris teaches these new and classical texts.  In this Alumni College class I would like to explore the reasons for these changes.  

Wesley Morris has a BA and MA in English from The University of Kentucky and a PhD in Literary Theory and English from The University of Iowa.  He has published several articles and reviews as well as three books: Toward a New Historicism, Friday’s Footprint, and Reading Faulkner.  Morris came to Rice from The University of Iowa in 1968 to teach literary theory and criticism, learned about William Faulkner from his colleague and Faulkner mentor, David Minter, and expanded his interests to Modern American Literature. He has served as Chair of the Department of English and Master of Hanszen College.

James Tour
Nanotechnology.  From nanocars to medicine

A Century in the making

An overview of the nanotechnology being developed in the presenter’s laboratory will be delivered.  This will include topics such as nanocars (single molecule cars), nano for cancer chemotherapy and brain injury, and nano for oil drilling and downhole oil detection. 

James M. Tour, a synthetic organic chemist, received his Bachelor of Science degree in chemistry from Syracuse University, his Ph.D. in synthetic organic and organometallic chemistry from Purdue University, and postdoctoral training in synthetic organic chemistry at the University of Wisconsin and Stanford University. After spending 11 years on the faculty of the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry at the University of South Carolina, he joined the Center for Nanoscale Science and Technology at Rice University in 1999 where he is presently the T. T. and W. F. Chao Professor of Chemistry, Professor of Computer Science, and Professor of Mechanical Engineering and Materials Science. Tour’s scientific research areas include nanoelectronics, graphene electronics, carbon nanovectors for medical applications, green carbon research for enhanced oil recovery and environmentally friendly oil and gas extraction, graphene photovoltaics, chemical self-assembly, flame retarding polymer additives, carbon nanotube and graphene synthetic modifications, carbon composites, hydrogen storage on nanoengineered carbon scaffolds, synthesis of single-molecule nanomachines which include molecular motors and nanocars, use of the NanoKids concept for K-12 education in nanoscale science, Dance Dance Revolution and Guitar Hero science educational package development for middle school education, and methods for retarding chemical terrorist attacks. Tour has over 400 research publications and 50 patents.

Reto Geiser
"2 OU 3 CHOSES QUE JE SAIS D'ELLE": Observations on Contemporary Architecture

A Century in the making

A closer look at contemporary architecture.

Reto Geiser is an architect and scholar with a focus in modern architecture and the contemporary architectural and urban discourse. He is currently the Wortham Assistant Professor at the Rice School of Architecture. He studied architecture at ETH Zurich and Columbia University in New York, and holds a PhD from ETH Zurich with a thesis investigating aspects of cultural transfer and transatlantic exchange in the work of the architectural historian Sigfried Giedion.

A founding principal of the collaborative design practice MG&Co., he is developing spatial strategies in a range of scales from the book to the house, exploring the boundaries of design and research with a special focus on the intersections and overlaps between architecture, installation, textiles and typography. His curatorial works include the exhibition "Explorations: Teaching, Design, Research," Switzerland’s official contribution to the 11th Venice Architecture Biennale. Geiser's writings have been published in anthologies and such publications as Abitare, Future Anterior, and Hunch. He is the editor of award-winning Explorations in Architecture (Birkhäuser, 2008), and the series editor of Standpunkte Magazine, and Standpunkte Dokumente.

John Boles
Edgar Odell Lovett and the Founding and Shaping of the Rice Institute

The Centennial

This lecture will discuss Lovett’s education, his teaching experience, his round-the-world trip, his charisma, and his leadership and show how he took William Marsh Rice vague charter and turned it into a blueprint for the creation of a great university, and then show his role and making this vision become reality.

John B. Boles, the William Pettus Hobby Professor of History at Rice University and editor of the Journal of Southern History, received his undergraduate training at Rice (BA, 1965) and his doctorate from the University of Virginia (PhD, 1969), where he was a Thomas Jefferson Foundation Fellow and a Woodrow Wilson Dissertation Fellow; he later held an N.E.H. postdoctoral fellowship in anthropology at Johns Hopkins University. He is the author of ten books—The Great Revival, 1787–1805:  Origins of the Southern Evangelical Mind (1972); Religion in Antebellum Kentucky (1976); Black Southerners, 1619–1869 (1983); Rice University and the 1990 Economic Summit of Industrialized Nations (1991); A University So Conceived:  A Brief History of Rice (1992); The Irony of Southern Religion (1994); and The South Through Time:  A History of an American Region (1995; 3rd ed., 2004), University Builder: Edgar Odell Lovett and the Founding of the Rice Institute (2007),and co-author of Unto a Good Land: A History of the American People (2005)—and the editor or coeditor of eleven other books, including Interpreting Southern History (1987), A Companion to the American South (2002), Shapers of Southern History: Autobiographical Reflections (2004), and most recently, Seeing Jefferson Anew: In His Time and Ours (2010).   He has also written many reviews and articles and spoken widely on history, editing, and teaching, and he directs a large graduate training program in southern history at Rice.

In 1991 he was awarded the Meritorious Service Award by the Association of Rice Alumni and in 2004 he received their Distinguished Alumni Award; in 1994 and 2004 he received the Graduate Student Association Teaching Award. He won the Presidential Mentoring Award in 2006 and the George R. Brown Award for Superior Teaching in 2007 and 2009. During the spring semester of 2000 he held the Robert Foster Cherry Distinguished Teaching Professorship at Baylor University; in the winter semester of 2005-6 he held the Fulbright-Leipzig Distinguished Chair in American Studies at the University of Leipzig, Germany; and in the winter semester of 2009-10 he held the Leibniz Professorship at the Institute of Advanced Study, University of Leipzig, Germany.  He is an associate of Will Rice College and a member of the Houston Philosophical Society, the Philosophical Society of Texas, and the Southern Historical Association.

John Hopkins
The Parthenon and Architectural Meaning

a Century in the Making

In this lecture I discuss some of the material from my course this semester on the Parthenon.  The course investigates not only the meaning that the Parthenon held for an ancient Athenian, but also, how later people in Athens (Romans, Ottomans, British diplomats) interpreted its significance.  I will close by questioning the meaning of the campus architecture of Rice and whether one can use some of the same tools for understanding the Parthenon to look at architecture in the modern world.

John Hopkins is ACLS New Faculty Fellow in the Department of Art History at Rice.  He specializes in the art and architecture of the ancient Mediterranean with a particular focus on Roman architecture and sculpture, spatial experience and cultural and societal change.  Much of his work incorporates the use of virtual technology for the reconstruction of ancient Rome, as a means to present as full a picture of the architecture of antiquity as possible to students and colleagues.  He is currently finishing a book manuscript on the genesis of Roman architecture and has previously published on the Capitoline Temple and on the creation of the Roman Forum. He is Fellow of the American Academy in Rome, the American Council of Learned Societies and the Getty Research Institute.

Faculty Panel on Diversity
Richard Tapia, Chandler Davidson, others TBA

A Century in the Making

This panel of faculty members will discuss the great strides toward equal rights in academic institutions for people of diverse ethnicity, women, and the LGBT community.

Richard Tapia joined Rice's faculty in 1970. He is a University Professor -- Rice’s highest rank -- and the Maxfield-Oshman Professor in Engineering and a professor of computational and applied mathematics. He is also director of Rice’s Center for Excellence and Equity in Education.

The son of Mexican immigrants, Tapia grew up in Los Angeles and was the first member of his family to attend college. He excelled in math and science and went on to earn international acclaim for his research into numerical optimization methods, and that success led to his election to the National Academy of Engineering. Tapia has authored or co-authored two books and more than 100 mathematical research papers, and he is currently authoring a graduate-level textbook on the foundations of optimization. Tapia has directed or co-directed more underrepresented minority and women doctoral students in mathematics than anyone else in the country. Due partly to his influence, Rice's Department of Computational and Applied Mathematics has graduated more than double the national average of both minority and female Ph.D. students for more than a decade.

Chandler Davidson, Research Professor and Tsanoff Chair of Public Affairs Emeritus, taught at Rice from 1966 to 2003. He was a founding member of the Department of Sociology and served as departmental chair for fourteen years between 1979 and 2003. In the latter part of his career, he had a joint appointment with the Department of Political Science. Davidson has won five university-wide teaching prizes, including Rice's top award, the George R. Brown Excellence in Teaching Prize. In addition to many articles appearing in academic journals and popular magazines, he has written or edited a number of books. In the early 1990s he and Professor Bernard Grofman of the University of California at Irvine directed a major scholarly effort to assess the impact of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 in the South. Funded by the National Science Foundation and the Rockefeller Foundation, the project involved almost thirty political scientists, historians, sociologists, and voting rights lawyers. The resulting book, Quiet Revolution in the South (Princeton University Press, 1994), was co-edited by Davidson and Grofman and won the Richard Fenno Prize awarded by the American Political Science Association for the best book published on legislative behavior that year. Davidson's scholarship on voting rights has been cited at least seven times in U.S. Supreme Court opinions and numerous times in lower court opinions. In a 2011 case before the U.S. District Court for the District Court of Columbia concerning whether Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act was still valid, the Court affirmatively cited two of his books as well as his 2006 U.S. Senate testimony a total of 27 times. He is also continuing research on minority voting rights. In 2005-06 Davidson was asked to serve on the ten-member National Commission on the Voting Rights Act as Congress prepared to consider reauthorizing the non-permanent provisions of the Act. In that capacity he was the primary drafter and author of the Commission's 2006 report, Protecting Minority Voters. He was also invited that year to testify before the U.S. Senate Judiciary Committee regarding the Act's reauthorization.  In 2011 he was asked by a civil rights organization, The Advancement Project, to present an affidavit to the U.S. Department of Justice regarding the history of racial discrimination in elections in Texas, the purpose of which affidavit was to question the motives and impact of Texas’ recently enacted law requiring a photo ID as a prerequisite to voting.
