What is Alumni College Weekend?

Since 1995, the Association of Rice Alumni has held this annual event inviting alumni, parents, and friends to campus for a uniquely Rice experience: a weekend of classes from our outstanding faculty.  We are one of the few institutions throughout the nation that can support continuing education in precisely this way.  The Association of Rice Alumni firmly believes that the pursuit of knowledge should continue beyond your time as a student, and Alumni College Weekend is a fabulous opportunity to sample the recent research and first-class teaching emerging from the university.  And when you’re not in class, Alumni College offers a chance to mix, mingle, and network with your fellow “classmates”— a broad spectrum of Rice alumni from various class years and regions, plus parents of current students and other knowledge-seeking friends.  Read through the course descriptions, decide which classes interest you most and sign up today!

John Anderson
Global Change Impact on Antarctica and Antarctica’s Contribution to Global Change

Biography of Earth

While debate continues concerning climate change and its causes, it is now certain that global warming in historical time has resulted in a measureable increase in the temperature of the global ocean and that, in turn, has resulted in a five-fold increase in the rate of sea-level rise.   The Antarctic Peninsula has experienced the most dramatic rise in temperature and that has resulted in unprecedented change in glaciers and ice shelves, as well as the ecosystems associated with these settings.  The big concern now is the impact of climate change on the larger ice streams that drain the ice sheet.  These ice streams are showing signs of instability and, if this trend continues, the consequences would be dramatic increases in sea level that will have a profound impact on coastal cities.

John Anderson joined the faculty at Rice University in 1975 and served as chairman of the Department of Earth Science from 1992 through 1998. Anderson has participated in 24 scientific expeditions to Antarctica, his research there focusing on the history of the ice sheet, glacial response to climate change and potential contributions to sea level rise. His other research focuses on the evolution of the Texas coast and on predicting coastal response to climate change this century.  He has authored two books Antarctic Marine Geology and The Formation and Future of the Upper Texas Coast, and has co-edited four books; he has most recently contributed to a Geological Society of America Special Paper entitled Response of Upper Gulf Coast Estuaries to Holocene Climate Change and Sea-Level Rise. He has published 195 peer-reviewed papers and mentored more than 50 graduate students. Anderson received the 1992 GCAGS Outstanding Educator Award, the 1996 Rice University Graduate Teaching Award, 2004 Rice University Presidential Mentoring Award, and was the 2007 recipient of the Society of Sedimentary Research Shepard Medal. He is a Fellow of the Geological Society of America, past president of the Society for Sedimentary Research, and academic director of the Shell Center for Sustainability.

Linda Neagley
The Bayeux Tapestry:  Telling the Tale of the Battle of Hastings

Arts and Letters

This lecture will discuss the most important pictorial narrative epic of the Middle Ages—the 230-foot Bayeux Tapestry that depicts the Battle of Hastings in 1066 and the events leading up to William’s crossing into to England. We will discuss how and why the tapestry presents an ambiguous perspective of the story and how medieval people might have read and experienced the imagery.

Suggested readings or recordings:
Bernstein, David J. The Mystery of the Bayeux Tapestry (University of Chicago Press, 1986)
CD-ROM: The Battle of Hastings 1066 - Tapestry of a Battle, narrated by Michael Leighton

Linda Elaine Neagley is associate professor and director of graduate studies at Rice University.  Her research interests include late gothic architecture and sculpture of northern Europe; Gothic design theory; late medieval urban planning; issues of vision and visuality in the Middle Ages; and medieval narrative and pictorial space.  She is the author of Disciplined Exuberance: The Parish Church of Saint-Maclou and Late Gothic Architecture in Rouen and has published articles in The Art Bulletin, Journal of the Society of Architectural Historians, and Gesta.  She is currently writing a chapter on late Gothic architecture for the Cambridge History of Medieval Architecture, and is beginning computer reconstruction of the medieval city of Rouen based on the extraordinary maps in the Livre des Fontaines. 
Professor Neagley received her PhD at Indiana University in 1983.   She came to Rice University in 1992 after having taught in the School of Architecture at Berkeley and in the art history departments of UCLA, University of Michigan, and Colgate University.  She was the director of three plan survey projects in France funded by the National Endowment of the Arts Interpretive Research Grants and the University of Michigan.

Architectural Method: The Education of an Architect

Anthony Brandt
Music and the Mind

Arts and Letters

The scientific exploration of the human mind is relatively young and brain imaging technology is very new.  Yet the arts and philosophy have been researching the mind for thousands of years — how we think; how we feel; what we notice, care about, and remember; how we search for and recognize patterns, and ascribe meaning to them.  Thus, a partnership between the science and the arts offers one of the richest ways to investigate the mind.  Because music is abstract, non-verbal, and universal to human culture, it has the potential to offer especially powerful insights into how we think, remember, and relate.

An associate professor of composition and theory at Rice’s Shepherd School of Music Anthony Brandt began his career with degrees from the California Institute of Arts and Harvard University.  He is co-founder and president of the acclaimed contemporary music ensemble Musiqa.  Brandt has received Rice’s Phi Beta Kappa Teaching Prize and the Access to Artistic Excellence Grant from the National Endowment for the Arts.

Beverly Mitchell
An American Perspective on the Practice of Circumcision

Biography of Earth

The practice of female circumcision poses challenges for our medical, legal, and other cultural institutions, while male circumcision remains deeply embedded in our culture with minimal critique. Circumcision is a challenging topic for anthropology, which promotes cultural relativism — understanding culture through the eyes of those in a given culture — without condoning or condemning the practices it strives to understand. Beverly Mitchell, PhD, lecturer and research associate, will address how the United States has reacted to immigrants from countries that practice female circumcision and discuss American views of male circumcision.

Beverly Mitchell, PhD, is a lecturer and research associate in anthropology at Rice University.  She completed all of her degrees in the anthropology department at Rice, receiving her BA in 1985, her MA in 2000, and her PhD in 2003.  Her research is founded in cultural anthropology and centers around the dichotomous ideology that has pitted cultural relativists against political activists aimed at eradicating the practice commonly known as female circumcision.

Living with floods and hurricanes in the Gulf Coast of Texas

He currently is the director of the Severe Storm Prediction Center (SSPEED) at Rice University which consists of a team of seven Gulf Coast universities and 15 investigators dedicated to improving storm prediction, education, and evacuation from disaster.

A Biological Basis for Human Behavior

Modern Science

This talk will present a century and a half of observation and controversy over the role of the brain in human and animal behavior and how nerves communicate signals, including evidence from experimentation and from observations of human behavior following brain injury, and finishing with an overview of the biological basis for perception.

Melissa Kean
Captain Baker's Institute: Rice before Lovett

Campus, Culture, and City

The Rice Institute was founded with a donation by William Marsh Rice in 1891. Although he claimed that he wanted no action taken with regard to the formation of the institution before his death, Mr. Rice, envisioning the creation of a school for vocational training, did in fact take some small steps toward its opening. After his murder in 1900, the Board of Trustees, led by Captain James A. Baker, was left to sort out what Rice's intentions were for the new institute, what resources were available, and what were the possibilities open to them in the rapidly changing landscape of American higher education. By the time they offered the presidency to Edgar Odell Lovett some seven years after Rice's death, their thinking had evolved in ways that allowed them to embrace Lovett's bold vision of Rice as a globally influential center of learning. This talk will explore how they got there.

Melissa Kean is the Rice University Centennial Historian.  A student of the history of American higher education, she has written histories of Rice’s School of Continuing Studies and the Jesse H. Jones Graduate School of Management. In 2000, she won Rice’s John W. Gardner Award for Best Dissertation in the schools of Humanities and Social Sciences. Her book, “The Desegregation of Private Universities in the South: Duke, Emory, Rice, Tulane and Vanderbilt” was published in 2008 by LSU Press. Kean’s current projects are a scholarly history of Rice University and a study of the collapse of social regulations on college campuses in the 1960s.

Understanding and Enjoying Music for Film

Arts and Letters

Motion pictures generally have a common musical structure, in one sense. This consists of an opening-titles piece, an underscore, and a closing-credits piece. For the audience, the most memorable music, that which they leave the theater humming or whose later hearing recalls the picture most vividly, is usually that of the main titles — the so-called theme music. But for the composer/practitioner, the real interest and challenge often lies more in the underscore….

Beyond Nano—The Structure of Matter at the FemtoScale and How it Shapes the Nature of Our Universe

Modern Science

Over the past 40 years, we have made great strides in our understanding of the structure of matter at the smallest scale, but our standard model leaves many questions unanswered.  This talk will review the historical development of the field of particle physics and our current understanding of the nature of matter at the femto-scale, with the discussion centered on the unanswered questions.  The close interplay of physics at the smallest scale and physics at the largest scale (astrophysics) will be described.

Brandon Dugan
The Earth Around Us

Biography of Earth

Over the 4.6 billion years of Earth’s history, complex interactions between its systems have yielded the environment in which we live. This includes feedbacks between rocks, water, atmosphere, and life. We will explore the basic evolution of these systems and the feedbacks between them with an emphasis on major events of the deep past and the recent past. We will conclude with thoughts on how the past tells us about the future.

Suggested readings or recordings:
Class participants are encouraged to scan recent news articles that discuss Earth science in action. Examples include global climate change, earthquakes, volcanoes, and the like. Many of these articles describe the events. We will discuss the science behind these events.

Brandon Dugan has been at Rice since 2005. Prior to starting as faculty of Earth science, Brandon earned a bachelor’s degree in geological engineering from the University of Minnesota, a doctorate in geosciences from Pennsylvania State University, and completed a postdoctoral fellowship with the US Geological Survey. Brandon’s research centers on how fluids move through the shallow Earth with applications to a range of problems encompassing freshwater resources, natural hazards, unconventional natural resources, and oil and gas exploration.

Christina Willis
Language and Culture

Biography of Earth

This lecture will explore some of the key topics addressed in studies of language and culture (also known as linguistic anthropology). We will begin by examining the notion of linguistic relativity, which suggests that our language shapes our thoughts. We will consider the implications of such an hypothesis on the concept of worldview. This will be followed by exploring how the relationship between language, culture and thought is explored through ethnographic and experimental research in linguistics and anthropology.

Christina Willis is an assistant professor of linguistics at Rice University. She received her doctorate from the University of Texas at Austin. Willis’ research centers on the issues surrounding language endangerment with an emphasis on linguistic description and documentation. Her primary methodology is a discourse-centered approach to data collection, which concentrates on naturally-occurring data for analysis. She is also interested in ethnographic approaches to documentation and description. Willis specializes in the Tibeto-Burman languages of the Himalayas; she has written a descriptive grammar of Darma, which is spoken in India by fewer than 2,600 people.

“Nothing,” to quote scientist and writer Jared Diamond, “is so fascinating or complicated as the trajectory of a human life.” The issues that developmental psychology is concerned with include stabilities and changes in life, whether the developmental trajectory takes one course or many, and the ever relevant question of nature versus nurture. Laura Segatori
Protein Misfolding Diseases: Pathogenicity and Therapeutic Perspectives

Modern Science

Among the building blocks of all living systems, proteins are versatile molecules with a remarkably broad variety of functions, including enzymatic, structural, regulatory, transport, and signaling. It is widely accepted that 3D structure and biological activity of a protein are very tightly linked, such that small structural changes can cause functional impairments leading to a cellular toxicity and, eventually, to development of devastating human diseases, collectively referred to as "protein misfolding diseases." By analyzing a few examples of loss of function diseases (lysosomal storage disorders) and gain of function diseases (Parkinson’s and Alzheimer’s disease), this talk will provide an overview of the current knowledge of protein misfolding in association with disease development and of the therapeutic perspectives for protein misfolding diseases.

Laura Segatori is the T.N. Law Assistant Professor of Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering at Rice University. She also holds joint appointments in the departments of bioengineering and biochemistry and cell biology at Rice.  She received her bachelors degree in biotechnology from the University of Bologna (Italy) in 2000 and her PhD in chemical engineering from the University of Texas at Austin in 2005. She was a postdoctoral research fellow at The Scripps Research Institute from 2005 to 2007. Segatori’s research focuses on cellular protein folding, particularly in association with the development of human diseases.

John T. McDevitt
Tools for Affordable Healthcare Based on Bio-Nano-Chips

Modern Science

In this presentation Dr. McDevitt will describe steps that are now being taken for the development of next-generation Nano-Bio-Chip Sensors. The key technology that will enable developments here is based on powerful medical micro-devices that are capable of sophisticated measurements of the molecules of life that are present in complex bio-fluid samples like blood, urine, and saliva. These Rice efforts have the potential to reduce health care-costs, revolutionize diagnostic medicine by creating the platform that enables personalized medicine to become a reality, create a new Texas nano-bioscience industry with strong economic impact, and establish Texas as the world’s leader in nano-bio-chip sensor systems.

John T. McDevitt joined the Rice faculty as the Brown-Wiess Professor of Bioengineering and Chemistry in 2009. He was previously at the University of Texas at Austin where he and his research group of 25 members established one of largest patent portfolios in the history of UT. The McDevitt laboratory at Rice focuses on the development of micro-medical devices that are fabricated with the same methods that are used to make the integrated circuit. These powerful yet affordable mini-sensor ensembles have the potential to reduce health care costs while at the same time improving treatment for patients suffering from cancer, stroke, cardiac and neurological diseases.  He earned a PhD in chemistry from Stanford University in 1987 and holds a BS in chemistry from California Polytechnic State University at San Luis Obispo. McDevitt completed a postdoctoral fellowship at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill before joining the UT faculty.

Stephen Klineberg
The Changing Face of Houston: Tracking the Economic and Demographic Transformations through 29 Years of Houston Surveys

Campus, Culture, and City

The Houston region recovered from the collapse of the oil boom in the 1980s to find itself squarely in the midst of a restructured economy and a demographic revolution. We will review the findings from almost 30 years of the "Houston Area Survey" (1982–2010) to measure these remarkable transformations and to consider their implications for the challenges and opportunities that lie ahead.

A graduate of Haverford College, with an MA from the University of Paris and a PhD from Harvard, Stephen Klineberg is professor of sociology at Rice University and co-director of the new Kinder Institute for Urban Research. The recipient of ten major teaching awards at Rice, including the Lifetime Award for Excellence in Teaching, he is the founding-director of the annual “Houston Area Survey,” now beginning its 30th year of tracking the remarkable changes in the population patterns, economic outlooks, experiences, and beliefs of Harris County residents.

William Parsons
Freud, Jung, and the Creation of Modern Spirituality

Biography of Earth

In recent years it has become fashionable to describe one’s relation to the divine as “spiritual but not religious.” This saying usually means that yes, one is “religious” but not in the usual manner of subscribing to a particular religious tradition or participating in its prescribed rituals. On the contrary, institutional religion is seen as the enemy, a place where authoritarianism, mendacity, and dogmatism have replaced authentic, living religious experience. Those professing modern spirituality are notably eclectic, adopting a supermarket approach to religious traditions and practices and constructing a highly individualized form of relating to the divine. Recent surveys estimate that more than 25% of those living in the United States profess this kind of spirituality. This lecture will explore the contours and nature of modern spirituality, focusing on how the rise of psychology, particularly those strands affiliated with the depth-psychological tradition of Freud and Jung, were and still are complicit in its cultural ascendancy.

William B. Parsons is associate professor of religious studies at Rice University. His publications include The Enigma of the Oceanic Feeling, Religion and Psychology: Mapping the Terrain, Mourning Religion, Disciplining Freud on Religion, Teaching Mysticism, and dozens of essays in multiple journals and edited books. He has served as chair of the Department of Religious Studies at Rice University, director of the Rice University Humanities Research Center, editor of Religious Studies Review, and has been a fellow at the Martin Marty Center of the University of Chicago and at the Institute for Advanced Studies (IAS) at Hebrew University.

André Droxler
The Future of Coral Reefs in a Warming Earth

Biography of Earth

Tropical corals reefs are considered to be equivalent to the rain forests of the oceans. Although they cover less than one percent of the global ocean sea floor, coral reefs contain the highest ocean biodiversity. In the past century, local, regional, and global anthropogenic activities, such as an increase of atmospheric CO2, have introduced increasing stresses on modern coral reefs at alarming rates. More than fifty percent of the tropical coral reefs are under threat to collapse in the next few decades; twenty percent have already been destroyed. Because of their sensitivity to environmental changes, coral reefs are considered the “canary in the mine shaft” for the world’s oceans. In the context of a warming Earth and the global decay of its environments, what is the future in the next century for coral reefs and the oceans in general?

The main focuses of his research include the regional and global evolution of coral reefs, paleo-oceanographic/climatic records, past sea level fluctuations archived in sediments deposited around reefs and carbonate platforms.

Vicki Colvin
Nanotechnology: An Emerging Industry

Modern Science

Nanotechnology-enabled systems offer great promise for solving difficult social problems and, consequently, enormous possibility for commerce.  Some of these innovations are related to the fundamental material properties at the heart of any nanotechnology.  Their small size, high surface areas, and unique properties render these materials useful to industries ranging from transportation to pharmaceuticals.  Recently, nanotechnology has been used to treat cancer, clean water, and increase independence from fossil fuels.  Yet there are larger social concerns about whether the new economies for manufacturing would disenfranchise poor people tied to the non-nano world.   This presentation will cover how these promising developments can help create a more sustainable and socially acceptable industry.

Vicki Colvin is the director of the Center for Biological and Environmental Nanotechnology (CBEN) and the co-director of the Richard E. Smalley Institute for Nanoscale Science and Technology at Rice University.  Currently she serves as the Kenneth S. Pitzer-Schlumberger Professor of Chemistry and professor of chemistry and chemical and biomolecular engineering.  Colvin studies how nanoscale particles interact with the environment and living systems.  Her research draws on both synthetic chemistry for the preparation and control of novel nanophase systems as well as physical chemistry for the investigation of their unusual behavior.  Colvin’s projects utilize the unique and responsive behavior of nanoparticles to solve problems related to water purification and targeted cell death.  Colvin received her bachelor’s degree in chemistry and physics from Stanford University, and her PhD in chemistry from the University of California, Berkeley.
