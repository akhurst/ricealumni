Rice University Alumni College Weekend
March 3–5, 2006

What is Alumni College Weekend?

Since 1995, the Association of Rice Alumni has held an annual event inviting alumni and friends back to campus for a weekend of classes from Rice's outstanding faculty. We firmly believe that the pursuit of knowledge should be lifelong, and Alumni College is a fabulous opportunity to sample the most recent research and top-notch teaching of the university. And when you're not in class, Alumni College offers you a chance to mingle at meals and social events with your fellow "classmates" — a broad spectrum of Rice alumni from various class years and regions, plus parents of current students and other knowledge-seeking friends. Read through the course descriptions, decide which classes interest you most, and sign up today!

The 2006 Program

The classes offered at Alumni College are grouped into "tracks" — themes that help you identify topics of particular interest. This year we are pleased to present the following tracks:

Interdisciplinary Centers and Institutes at Rice — Hear from leaders of some of the centers and institutes promoting interdisciplinary research and collaboration.

Around the World in 48 Hours — Take a journey around the globe with courses in international history, art, and literature.

Modern Classics — Explore the modern era with classes in twentieth- and twenty-first-century literature, art, and music. 

Architecture — Learn more about the progressive work and research of the nationally renowned Rice School of Architecture. 

Ethics — Gain insight into different viewpoints of ethics through religious and cultural perspectives. 

Online registration is available through March 1. For more information on the Alumni College program, please contact Lauren Linn at 713-348-6093.

  • Paris Then and Now, Deborah Nelson-Campbell
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    This class will present an overview of the history of Paris, from pre-history to the present day, to create an understanding of the development of the city as a capital and as a cultural, intellectual, and economic center. Many familiar monuments in the city have relatively unknown stories while many other sites exist as hidden gems. This "most beautiful city in the world" offers something for everyone.

    Suggested readings:
    Ernest Hemingway, A Movable Feast (1964).
    Michelin Guide Paris (any edition).
    Steven Barclay, ed., A Place in the World Called Paris (2002).

    Follow-up readings:
    Alex Karmel, A Corner in the Marais (1998).

    Deborah Nelson-Campbell is a professor of French and director of Rice's Center for the Study of Languages and has taught at Rice since 1974. She has lived and worked in Paris and returns every year at least once for work-related activities. A scholar of medieval French language and literature, she has published two critical editions of medieval poetry, plus a critical bibliography and numerous articles on courtly narrative lyric poetry. Separately, she has also published an edition of her grandmother's journal, documenting her life in rural Kentucky from 1898–1900.
     

  • Business Ethics, Duane Windsor
    		•

    Ethics

    This class will address business ethics in the wake of recent notorious corporate scandals (Enron, Tyco, WorldCom, etc.) and the resulting passage of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. For long-term stability, the best business strategy is to be ethical, but there are strong financial incentives for individual misconduct. How do bad judgments, rogue executives, corrupt corporate cultures, and corrupt operating environments abroad affect a business? How can executives design and implement a strongly ethical climate within a business?

    Suggested readings:
    Kristen Hays, "Lay-Skilling Trial Is Really About Lying," Associated Press, January 20, 2006. Available online.
    Michael Jensen, "Paying People to Lie: The Truth About the Budgeting Process." Available online.

    Follow-up readings:
    Sumantra Ghoshal, "Bad Management Theories Are Destroying Good Management Practices," Academy of Management Learning & Education (March 2005).
    Sherron S. Watkins, "Ethical Conflicts at Enron: Moral Responsibility in Corporate Capitalism," California Management Review (July 2003).
    Simon Zadek, "The Path to Corporate Social Responsibility," Harvard Business Review (December 2004).

    Duane Windsor is the Lynette S. Autrey Professor of Management in the Jesse H. Jones Graduate School of Management. He has been a member of the Rice faculty since 1977. He presently teaches courses in leadership, business ethics, and public and nonprofit management. Windsor conducts active research concerning business ethics and corporate social responsibility. Raised in Houston, he earned his BA from Rice in 1969 and holds a PhD in political economy and government from Harvard University.
     

  • In Defense of Tolerance: The Mission of the Boniuk Center, Carol Quillen
    		•

    Interdisciplinary Centers and Institutes at Rice

    Tolerance as an ideal finds few strong advocates. Those who value religious diversity argue that we should delight in and learn from our differences, rather than simply tolerate them. For others, tolerance means condoning acts they know to be wrong or beliefs they know to be false. This class will explore how a rich understanding of and an active commitment to tolerance can foster peaceful co-existence among persons of profoundly different religious backgrounds.

    Suggested readings:
    Michael Walzer, On Toleration (1999).
    E.J. Dionne, Jean Bethke Elshtain, and Kayla Drogosz, eds., One Electorate Under God?
    A Dialogue on Religion and American Politics     (2004).
    Larry Diamond, Marc Plattner, and Philip Costopoulos, eds., World Religions and
    Democracy     (2005).

    Carol Quillen, an associate professor of history and director of Rice's Boniuk Center for the Study and Advancement of Religious Tolerance, was born in New York and grew up in Delaware, where she attended the Wilmington Friends School. She holds an AB in history from the University of Chicago and a PhD from Princeton University. Quillen is the author of the books Rereading the Renaissance: Petrarch, Augustine and the Language of Humanism and Petrarch's Secret, as well as numerous articles. Her scholarly research focuses on European humanism and on feminist political theory.
     

  • Installation Art: A Room of Their Own, Kimberly Davenport
    		•

    Modern Classics

    Rice is home to the only university gallery in the country dedicated to installation art. Unlike individual art objects, installation art is created in relation to the space it inhabits. It is, quite literally, "art you can get into." Though many think of this artistic genre as a new art form, "installation art" is a late 1960s term for a way that many artists have worked throughout art history. This class will review historical examples of installation art; examine why the Rice Gallery adopted its unique mission; and discuss how, given a room of their own, artists have created an extraordinary variety of memorable installations.

    Kimberly Davenport has been director of the Rice University Art Gallery since 1994. Under her leadership, the Rice Gallery has earned a national reputation for high-quality, cutting-edge installation art. Davenport has initiated collaborations within the university and with other institutions, including the School of Architecture and The Museum of Fine Arts, Houston. As a member of the National Register of Peer Professionals, Davenport assists the federal government's Design Excellence Program in selecting artists to receive commissions for the nation's important buildings. In October 2003, the Houston Press named her Houston's best curator, and in 2005 it named the Rice Gallery as best gallery.

  • Archiving the Americas: New Histories of the American Hemisphere, Caroline F. Levander
    		•

    Interdisciplinary Centers and Institutes at Rice

    The field of American studies is in a state of conceptual transformation. Many prominent scholars are responding to this challenge by adopting a comparative, hemispheric approach that is gradually reorganizing the fields of literature, history, and political science. In order to broaden and deepen the analysis of the cultures of the Americas, Rice has begun to compile an Americas archive. This class will review documents such as the first edition of the Chilean Constitution (1822) and Andrew Jackson's Claims Upon Mexico (1837), a key document to the Mexican-American War of 1848.

    Caroline F. Levander is the director of Rice's Center for the Study of Cultures and an associate professor of English. She earned undergraduate and graduate degrees from Rice, including a PhD in 1995. She is the author of multiple books and articles, most recently Hemisphere and Nation: American Literary and Cultural Geographies and Cradle of Liberty: Race, the Child, and National Belonging from Thomas Jefferson to W.E.B. Du Bois. She is currently at work on a book entitled Global Nations/Foreign Relations in the American Hemisphere.
     

  • Monet and the Impressionists at Argenteuil, Leo Costello
    		•

    Modern Classics

    The Parisian suburb of Argenteuil has secured a place in art history as perhaps the single most important site of Impressionism. It was here during the 1870s that Claude Monet and his colleagues came to artistic and professional maturity, creating some of the most enduring masterpieces of the Impressionist movement. This class will explore the pictorial, personal, and historical significance of this site. In particular, we will look at the work of Monet, noting the importance of Argenteuil as a varied motif for his work and a stable base for his personal and professional aspirations.

    Leo Costello is an assistant professor of art history at Rice, where he teaches courses in European art. He received his PhD from Bryn Mawr College in 2002, having focused his research on the contemporary history paintings of J.M.W. Turner. His article on Turner's painting Slave Ship appeared recently in Discourses of Slavery and Abolition, an anthology published by Palgrave MacMillan. He is currently at work on an article on Turner's painting The Battle of Trafalgar. He has also published on twentieth-century and contemporary art.

  • Music to Your Ears: What Makes Twentieth-Century Music Vital and Relevant and How to Listen to It, Anthony Brandt
    		•

    Modern Classics

    Progressive modern music of the past century is much more isolated and less publicly accepted than modern forms of ballet, theater, fiction, poetry, or film. A disconnection exists in the public mind between older forms of music and today's evolved styles. This lecture will discuss how contemporary music echoes conceptual shifts in science, society, and the other arts; explore the differences between classical and modern music; and help you listen with open ears and a curious mind to the new and unfamiliar.   • Urban Black Hole: Agglomeration of Critical Masses in Urban China, Clover Lee
    		•

    Architecture

    In contrast to the dispersed model of horizontal development typical in North America, the hyper-dense, agglomerated (networked) cities of China emerge as a dynamic alternative model — one which can no longer be governed by master plans but instead needs to be redefined by constantly changing physical and economic forces. But with the speed of urban growth three times that of worldwide populations, China is rapidly approaching its threshold of critical mass — the boundary when an urban model entails a new state as it reaches its optimum size. This class will discuss how architecture design necessitates the conception of new building typologies and urban forms to accommodate this proliferation of urban expansion and densification.

    Assistant professor Clover Lee received her bachelor of architecture degree from Cornell University and a master's from the Harvard University Graduate School of Design. She is currently the principal of plusClover, a design firm based in Los Angeles and Houston. Lee previously taught architecture at the University of Southern California and the University of California, Los Angeles. Lee currently is working on a collaborative research program between Rice's School of Architecture and Tongji University's College of Architecture and Urban Planning.
     

  • The Impact of Ancient Asian Yogic Practices in Contemporary Western Medical Settings, M. Alejandro Chaoul
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    Popular interest in Eastern healing and wellness practices has attracted, especially in recent years, the attention of Western science and academia. In fact, the emerging field of complementary and integrative medicine is rapidly adopting many of these Asian traditional methods. This class will explore the incorporation into Western medicine of the mind-body practices of India, China, and Tibet, with emphasis on the latter. Is this a trend that is here to stay?

    Religious studies doctoral candidate M. Alejandro Chaoul was born in Buenos Aires, Argentina. Chaoul is currently writing his dissertation on Tibetan mind-body practices and applications with cancer patients, conducting most of his research in Tibetan monastic communities in exile in India and Nepal. Chaoul has been teaching Tibetan meditation and mind-body techniques under the auspices of the Ligmincha Institute in various parts of the United States and Mexico since 1995. He has taught at Rice's School of Continuing Studies, the University of Houston, and the Jung Center of Houston. He is also a mind/body intervention specialist at the University of Texas M.D. Anderson Cancer Center's Integrative Medicine Program, where he facilitates meditation for cancer patients and their support system.

  • When the Chips are Down: The Grand Inquisitor and Father Zossima in Fyodor Dostoevsky's The Brothers Karamazov, Ewa M. Thompson
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    The Brothers Karamazov is an atypical nineteenth-century novel. Instead of presenting a standard tale of social climbing, Dostoevsky tackles such metaphysical questions as the existence of God and the right of human beings to conduct themselves as they wish. Within the novel, different characters come to different conclusions about these issues, but the grand debate becomes most interesting when Ivan Karamazov tells his tale of the Grand Inquisitor, and the saintly monk Zossima offers an indirect rejoinder. Dostoevsky allows the reader to take either side. This class will reveal how his own preference is embedded in the structure of the novel.

    Suggested readings:
    Fyodor Dostoyevsky, "Rebellion," "The Grand Inquisitor," "Notes of the Life in God of the deceased Priest and Monk, the Elder Zossima, taken from his own words by Alexey Fyodorovich Karamazov," and "Conversations and Exhortations of Father Zossima," in The Brothers Karamazov (any edition).

    Follow-up readings:
    George Panichas, The Burden of Vision: Dostoevsky's Spiritual Art (1985).
    Michael Holquist, Dostoevsky and the Novel (1977).
    W.J. Leatherbarrow, ed., The Cambridge Companion to Dostoevskii (2002).

    Ewa M. Thompson is a professor of Slavic studies and former chair of the Department of German and Slavic Studies at Rice. Born in Lithuania, she received her undergraduate education in Poland and her PhD from Vanderbilt University. She previously taught at Indiana University and at the University of Virginia, and was twice a visiting fellow at the Hoover Institution at Stanford. Thompson is the author of five books and has published articles in numerous scholarly and popular journals including Slavic Review, Slavic and East European Journal, and Modern Age. She is also the editor and founder of a tri-quarterly on Central and Eastern European affairs, The Sarmatian Review. Thompson received the Will Herberg Award from the Intercollegiate Studies Institute in 2003 and a year later was given the Turzanski Foundation Prize for scholarly achievement.

  • Byzantine-Latin Influence in Eastern Europe, Malgorzata Dabrowska
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    Although what we think of as the Roman Empire collapsed in 476 with the abdication of Romulus Augustus, the eastern half of the empire continued to thrive for another thousand years. Centered in Constantinople, the Byzantine Empire spread its influence not only throughout Eastern Europe, but across the entire medieval world. This class will examine the cultural impact of this Eastern Roman Empire on modern Eastern European society.

    Malgorzata Dabrowska is a visiting associate professor in German and Slavic studies. She received her education at the University of Łódź in Poland, earning her PhD in 1985 and completing the Habilitation in 1996. She has taught at the University of Łódź since 1985. Dabrowska is a member of the Polish Historical Society and the International Association of Byzantine Studies. Her research focuses on Polish contemporary history, Byzantine history, and modern Polish poetry.
     

  • Reading Twentieth-Century Argentine Short Fiction: Secret Miracles, Sudden Worlds in Borges and Cortázar, Lane Kauffmann
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    In their stories, Argentine authors Jorge Luis Borges and Julio Cortázar attempt to transcend regional and national identities through the adoption — by themselves and by their fictional characters — of transnational, cosmopolitan perspectives. Not all attempts at transcendence are successful, leaving an ironic or tragic contrast between the attempt and the result. In the fantastical fiction of Borges and Cortázar, local projects often create ironic or allegorical distancing, allowing readers to reflect on ethical, ideological, and philosophical issues relevant to the contemporary world. This class will review examples of this contrast between the cosmopolitan and the local, paying specific attention to Borges' "The Garden of Forking Paths" and "Emma Zunz" and to Cortázar's "Axolotl" and "Blow-up."

    Suggested readings:
    Jorge Luis Borges, "The Garden of Forking Paths," "Emma Zunz," "The Secret Miracle," and "Pierre Menard, Author of Don Quijote," in Labyrinths: Selected Stories and Other Writings, ed. James E. Irby and Donald A. Yates.
    Jorge Luis Borges, "The Aleph," in The Aleph and Other Stories, ed. Andrew Hurley.
    Julio Cortázar, "Axolotl," "Blow-up," and "The Continuity of Parks," in Blow-up and Other Stories.

    Follow-up readings:
    Roberto González Echevarría, ed., The Oxford Book of Latin American Short Stories (1997).

    Associate professor of Spanish Lane Kauffmann lived in Peru and Colombia between the ages of 9 and 13, sparking a lifelong interest in Latin America. He earned a BA in Romance languages and literatures at Princeton University in 1970. Following military service as a linguist in Vietnam, he earned a PhD in comparative literature at the University of California, San Diego in 1981. He has taught modern Spanish literature, humanities, and literary theory at Rice since 1976. His articles on topics including the European essay, modern literary criticism, and stories by Julio Cortázar have been published in collections and journals in the United States, Canada, Latin America, and Europe. He currently is working on a study of the modern Spanish essay and is co-editing a volume of essays on "Reinventing Hispanism" with Beatriz González-Stephan, a colleague at Rice.
     

  • War, Morality, and Modernity, Christian J. Emden
    		•

    Ethics

    While much recent discussion about the nature of war is limited to questions concerned with public policy, it is often forgotten that — especially in the European tradition — thinking about war is also part of wider cultural and intellectual processes on the path to modernity. This lecture will focus on the changes that the cultural perception and intellectual discussion of war and organized violence have undergone, drawing on both political philosophy and media studies.

    Suggested readings:
    Thomas Aquinas, "War and Killing," in On Law, Morality, and Politics, trans. Richard J. Regan, ed. William P. Baumgarth and Richard J. Regan (2002), 164–72.
    Michael Howard, War in European History (2001 revised edition).
    Immanuel Kant, "Perpetual Peace: A Philosophical Sketch," in Political Writings, trans. H.B. Nisbet, ed. H.S. Reiss (2003), 93–130.
    Peter Paret, "Military Power," in Understanding War: Essays on Clausewitz and the History of Military Power (1992), 9–25.
    Carl Schmitt, The Concept of the Political, trans. George Schwab (1996).
    Paul Virilio, War and Cinema: The Logistics of Perception, trans. Patrick Camiller (1989).

    Christian J. Emden is an assistant professor of German at Rice. Educated at the University of Konstanz, Germany, and the University of Cambridge, England, his main area of research is German intellectual history since 1750. He is the author of Nietzsche on Language, Consciousness, and the Body and has co-edited three books. He is a founding member of the international research group Cultural History and Literary Imagination and co-editor of a book series connected to this research group. At Rice, he co-directs Focus Europe, a new undergraduate program in the humanities, and Changing Perceptions of the Public Sphere, a two-year collaboration between the Rice School of Humanities and the University of Cambridge.

  • Teaching the City: Houston and its Architecture, Stephen Fox
    		•

    Architecture

    By examining the architectural history of a city, we can connect historical events and personalities to buildings in ways that make general history more comprehensible and focused. Even in a city such as Houston, which is often regarded as lacking in both history and architecture, patterns and circumstances prevail in specific places. This class will present the story of Houston through a condensed and illustrated overview of the city's urban development and architectural history.

    Suggested readings:
    Stephen Fox, Houston Architectural Guide (1999).
    Stephen Fox, Rice University: An Architectural Tour (2001).

    Adjunct lecturer and Rice alumnus Stephen Fox is an architectural historian and a fellow of the Anchorage Foundation of Texas. He writes extensively about the architecture of Houston, its international influences, and the need to preserve Houston neighborhoods. Fox has led a number of architectural history tours for the Rice Design Alliance and serves on the advisory board of Architecture and Urbanism in Las Americas. His special interests range beyond Houston to the architecture of Galveston and the Texas-Mexico border.

    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    Was the book just a temporary Western mode of recording its culture? Will the printed codex be supplanted? Computers and lenses now allow us to see and communicate phenomena that exceed the capacity of printed texts to register. This class will explore the ways in which the book has both circulated and circumscribed knowledge.

    Robert L. Patten is the Lynette S. Autrey Professor in Humanities at Rice, and since 1977 has been editor of SEL Studies in English Literature 1500-1900. He has received numerous fellowships, including a Fulbright, a Guggenheim, and three from the National Endowment for the Humanities. Patten's two-volume book George Cruikshank's Life, Times, and Art was named outstanding biography of the 1990s by the London newspaper The Guardian. Patten has received five Rice teaching awards and four service awards. He has served on numerous university committees, was chair of the Department of English, was speaker of the Faculty Council from 2000–2002, and served on the most recent Rice presidential search committee.
     

  • Three Masterpieces of Mexican Literature, Maarten van Delden
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    In the 1950s and early '60s, a period during which Mexico was experiencing rapid modernization, three young novelists — Juan Rulfo, Rosario Castellanos, and Carlos Fuentes — burst onto the nation's literary scene with works characterized by their bold innovations in prose style and narrative form and by their highly charged explorations of Mexico's conflict-ridden history. This class will explore the tension between tradition and modernity as these authors sought to reflect both the immense change in Mexican society since the Mexican Revolution of 1910 and how the nation continued to be haunted by the unresolved traumas of the past.

    Suggested readings:
    Rosario Castellanos, The Book of Lamentations (1962).

    Interdisciplinary Centers and Institutes at Rice

    Rice's Center for Excellence in Tissue Engineering (CETE) focuses on the science and technology of tissue engineering and the development of new means of replacing damaged or diseased body parts and restoring function. His research has focused on the synthesis of new biomaterials for tissue engineering as carriers for controlled drug delivery and as non-viral vectors for gene therapy. He is the author of more than 270 articles and publications, he holds 19 patents, and he is the editor of seven books. Mikos is a founding editor of the journal Tissue Engineering.

  • The Real Leonardo da Vinci, Joseph Manca
    		•

    Around the World in 48 Hours

    From Renaissance gossip, to Sigmund Freud's analyses, to The Da Vinci Code, there has been a great amount of myth and misinformation about the life and art of Leonardo da Vinci. We will separate fact from fiction in reviewing his artistic output. Are his works full of secret codes? Is the Mona Lisa actually Leonardo da Vinci in drag? Was he a reclusive genius? In a richly illustrated slide lecture, come hear the truth about this great Italian master.

    Joseph Manca is a professor of art history and chair of the Department of Art History at Rice. He earned his PhD at Columbia University in 1986 and joined the faculty at Rice in 1989. He has published books on Italian Renaissance topics for the Cambridge University Press, Oxford University Press, and the National Gallery of Art in Washington, D.C. In addition, he has written more than 30 articles on Italian Renaissance and Early American topics.

    Modern Classics

    There are more than seventy-five versions of The Three Little Pigs available today, but are they all alike? What if the three pigs broke free of their story and wandered into other tales?
